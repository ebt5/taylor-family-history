## A Scottish Heritage: Royal Blood and Humble Beginnings

William Athol McMaster was born in Greenock, Renfrewshire, Scotland on 15 September 1816 — a port city on the Firth of Clyde, known for its shipbuilding and commerce. He was the son of Peter McMaster and Grace Henderson. Family tradition holds that William was **a descendant of King Robert the Bruce of Scotland**, a claim carried with pride through generations of the family.

His father Peter McMaster, however, proved a tragic figure. Peter married Grace Henderson on 22 July 1818 and they had five children — three boys and two girls. Then Peter deserted his family. Family oral history preserved the story: Peter and Grace had a quarrel at dinner one evening; Grace threw something at him, and Peter walked out and never returned. Grace was left to raise her children alone. She eventually became acquainted with the missionaries of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints and was baptized.

William came of age in this fatherless household, learning self-reliance early. His mother Grace's faith would shape the family's entire future.

---

## Margaret Drummond Ferguson: Hills and Faith

Margaret Drummond Ferguson was born 15 May 1821 in Greenock, Renfrewshire, to John Ferguson and Margaret Drummond. "Her childhood days were spent among the hills and dales of her native land," as one family history records. She grew up amid the rolling scenery of western Scotland, in a community that prized hard work and community ties.

William and Margaret married 2 September 1842. They would build a life together shaped by faith, exile, and the long westward journey to Utah.

---

## The Gospel Comes to Scotland

Elder Samuel Mulliner and Alexander Wright arrived in Glasgow as the first Latter-day Saint missionaries to Scotland. The McMaster family was among those who heard and accepted the restored gospel. William Athol McMaster was baptized and ordained to the Elders' quorum. His missionary calling would take him far.

According to a letter William wrote to his mother in January 1853 from "Harry-hayunat-Lane, Clitheroe, England," he had been serving as a traveling elder for two years: *"I am a traveling Elder going to and fro to lay before the children of men the Gospel of Jesus Christ which was restored to the Prophet Joseph of the Last Days, and as such I have very hard getting along, like my Master not where to lay my head. I have been stoned, mobbed and all manner of evil spoken against."*

Despite persecution, William served faithfully. He was a missionary to Great Britain from 1865 to 1869, years after his initial conversion and emigration.

---

## Children Born in Scotland

Several children were born to William and Margaret in Scotland, including:
- **John Brigham McMaster** (born 17 August 1843, Dunfermline, Fife) — baptized 25 August 1851
- **Virginia Faithful McMaster** (born 9 July 1850, Dunfermline, Fife) — Erik's ancestral line
- **James Bruce McMaster** (born 1 July 1859, Salt Lake City) — died 22 December 1862 in Salt Lake City

Virginia Faithful's name was a reflection of her father's missionary spirit. William's journal entry for her birth on 9 July 1850 recorded that the name "Virginia Faithful" was chosen *"because I was laboring in the Lord's vineyard at the time."*

---

## Emigration to America: The John M. Wood, 1854

William Athol McMaster, Margaret, Virginia Faithful, John Brigham, and other family members were among a large company of Saints who immigrated to America. They departed Liverpool on 12 March 1854 aboard the **John M. Wood**, arriving in New Orleans on 2 May 1854. William Athol arrived in Utah on **1 October 1854** with the Daniel Garn Overland Company — one of the organized wagon companies of the 1854 emigration season.

They settled in Salt Lake City, where William would spend the rest of his life.

---

## Life in Salt Lake: Rope-Maker, Merchant, Bishop's Counselor

William Athol McMaster became one of the prominent men of early Salt Lake City. The *Biographical Record of Salt Lake City and Vicinity* and the volume *Pioneers and Prominent Men of Utah* both document his contributions:

- **First rope-maker in Utah** — William established rope-making, a critical frontier industry
- **Merchant** in Salt Lake City
- **Member of the 11th Ward bishopric**
- **First superintendent of the 11th Ward Sunday School**
- **Missionary to Great Britain, 1865–1869**

From *Pioneers and Prominent Men of Utah*: "Family house: Salt Lake City. Missionary to Great Britain 1865-69; member 11th Ward bishopric; first superintendent of 11th ward Sunday school. First rope-maker in Utah. Merchant. Died Jan 20, 1887, Salt Lake City."

*(Note: records vary slightly on his death date — some say 20 January, the GEDCOM records 22 January 1887.)*

His son Alexander McMaster, born 12 August 1857 in Salt Lake City, became a prominent figure in his own right, whose biography was published in the *Biographical Record of Salt Lake City and Vicinity*.

---

## Margaret: The Steady Foundation

Margaret Drummond Ferguson McMaster remained in Salt Lake City after William's death in 1887, living until 21 April 1894. She had made the crossing from Scotland, endured frontier hardships, and raised a family that deeply shaped Utah's LDS community. Her daughter Virginia Faithful McMaster, who married William Duncombe Major, carried the line forward into Erik Taylor's ancestry.

William Athol McMaster was 70 years old when he died. Margaret followed seven years later at approximately 72 years of age.

---

## Legacy

Through their daughter Virginia Faithful McMaster (1850–1904), who married William Duncombe Major (1847–1925), William and Margaret became great-great-great-grandparents of Erik Taylor. Virginia and William raised a family in the Latter-day Saint tradition, including daughter Harriet Major Harrod, and their descendants spread across Utah.

The McMaster family history also carries the poignant story of Peter McMaster's desertion — Grace Henderson's courage in raising her children alone and embracing the gospel — as a reminder that this family's journey to America was born not just of faith, but of a woman's quiet determination.
