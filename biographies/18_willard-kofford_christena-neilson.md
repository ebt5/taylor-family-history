# Willard Kofford & Christena Neilson
## Biography #18

**Relationship to Erik Taylor:** Great-great-grandparents on the Kofford side. Their son **Willard Hogan Kofford** (Biography #20) married Veda Meiling, whose daughter Veda Alene Kofford married Paul Hector Taylor, making Willard and Christena the great-great-grandparents of Erik Taylor (through Erik's father Byron Taylor's mother's side).

Line: Erik → Byron Taylor → Veda Alene Kofford → Willard Hogan Kofford → **Willard Kofford & Christena Neilson**

## Two Converts from Scandinavia

Willard Kofford and Christena Neilson represent the Scandinavian branch of Erik's heritage — a man from the island of Bornholm in Denmark, and a woman from southern Sweden, who both emigrated to Utah as Latter-day Saint converts and built their family in Sanpete County.

Willard's name is a genealogist's puzzle. In Danish records he appears as **Janus Villard Kofoed** (or Larsen or Busk, depending on the record — Danish naming conventions of the era changed surnames with each generation). He was never formally christened. Upon arrival in America, he went by **Willard Kofford** — a stable anglicization that became the family name for all descendants. His citizenship was recorded on 5 October 1891 in Provo, Utah.

Christena Neilson emigrated from Sweden to Utah in approximately 1862, likely as a young child of six, as part of the LDS gathering from Scandinavia. She is recorded in the Sweden Household Clerical Survey (1654–1901) and in Utah census records.

---

## Life in Sanpete County

The Kofford family settled in the area of **Mount Pleasant** and the hay fields near Chester, Sanpete County — agricultural country in central Utah that had drawn many Scandinavian converts. Willard ran a dairy farm about three miles west of Chester, then later opened a grocery store in Aurora when the railroad was being extended through the area.

Their home was a one-room structure that became the site of a legendary family story, preserved in the history of their son Clarence Louis Kofford:

> *"While living in the one room the boys had to sleep on the floor. Their beds were rolled out each night. Each morning it was rolled up and laid out beside the house. One night Bill rolled the bed out and there lay a big blow snake. He called to his Mother to bring the poker. Not knowing there was a snake in the house she told him to get the stove poker himself. He told her fine, but to watch the snake while he got it. She quickly brought him the poker."*

## Children

Two sons are prominently documented:
1. **Willard Hogan Kofford** (born 26 September 1884, Mount Pleasant) — Erik's great-grandfather; later known as "Bill"
2. **Clarence Louis Kofford** (born 31 July 1887, Mount Pleasant) — delivered by Aunt Clara Kofford and a midwife, Mrs. Larsen; named Clarence after Aunt Clara, and Louis after grandfather Lars/Louis Kofford

A family photograph shows Willard and Christena Kofford with their two sons, young Willard Hogan and Clarence Louis, taken in the Sanpete years.

---

## Aurora, Provo Bench, and Orem

In spring 1896, Willard loaded all family belongings into wagons and the Kofford family headed north from Sanpete. They stopped briefly in Goshen where Willard wanted to buy land, but could never secure clear title. The family continued northward. 

When they reached Provo Bench, Willard recognized fruit-growing potential and made a decisive investment: he purchased **five acres of ground for $125.00 at 279 North State Street**, built a one-room home, and later added another room. He then bought **40 acres** where Orem City Hall and park now stand, later trading that for **20 acres** located between 3rd and 4th North and Main Street to 200 East in Orem. Willard planted peaches on much of this land — part of the Provo Bench fruit boom that made the area famous.

---

## Divorce and Later Years

The Kofford marriage did not endure. On 10 November 1908, a divorce decree was granted to Willard Kofford from Christena Kofford. On 3 December 1908 — less than a month after the divorce — Willard married **Carrie Lindsey** in Salt Lake City (marriage license #7384).

Christena remained in the area. The 1910 census shows her living by her son William Hogan Kofford, listed as a widow (though she was divorced, not widowed). Two boarders lived with her: a widow named Hilda Coates and her uncle, Hans Nielsen.

Christena Neilson Kofford died 18 March 1916 in Orem, Utah, at age 60. Her obituary was published in the *Daily Herald* (Provo) on Monday, 20 March 1916. She was buried 21 March 1916 in Provo. The *Provo Post* also carried a notice: "Mrs. Kofford Died at Sharon Sunday."

A Life History of Christena Neilson Kofford was preserved in family records (FamilySearch document TH-300-45496-113-13), as was a *Life of Christena Neilson Kofford* (TH-7767-126353-2116-10).

Willard Kofford (senior) lived on in Orem until 18 January 1939, dying at approximately 80 years of age. His obituary, naturalization papers, and marriage records are all preserved in the FamilySearch database. His funeral records and the notice of his death — "Willard Kofford Dies at Orem" — are among the family's preserved documents.

---

## Legacy

This Danish-Swedish couple represents the breadth of Latter-day Saint conversion across 19th-century Scandinavia — a movement that brought thousands of converts from Denmark, Sweden, and Norway to the Utah valleys. Their son Willard Hogan Kofford grew up on the family's peach orchards on the Provo Bench, married Veda Meiling of Mount Pleasant, and their daughter Veda Alene Kofford became Paul Hector Taylor's wife — bringing the Kofford line into direct connection with the Taylor family and ultimately to Erik.

The 40 acres where Orem City Hall now stands was once Willard Kofford's peach orchard. In a small way, a piece of modern Orem grew from the soil this Bornholm immigrant tended.
