## William Weldon Taylor

**Born:** 9 December 1883, Provo, Utah Territory  
**Died:** 24 November 1938, Provo, Utah  
**Parents:** William Joseph Taylor Jr. and Abby Jane Scott

William Weldon Taylor was born into the thick of the old Taylor household in Provo — large, religious, disciplined, and permanently busy. He was the eldest son of **William Joseph Taylor Jr.** and **Abby Jane Scott Taylor**, and by the look of later family memory he inherited both their strengths and their burdens: public duty, seriousness, deep family feeling, and the expectation that a man's life ought to be useful.

The memorial language written after his death is unusually intense. It calls his life "rich, pure," and above all a life "of service." Such writing can be formulaic, but in William's case the other family sources support it. He seems to have been one of those men who was always being drawn outward — into church work, community responsibility, business concerns, and the welfare of others — even when the claims of home were already heavy enough.

### A man his children called Papa

One of the most valuable sources is the 1983 set of recollections written by his children. Even in fragmentary form, those memories change the feel of the biography. They bring William down from memorial language into the house.

His daughter **Virginia Taylor Worthington** begins simply: **"I Remember Papa."** She adds that this is what she called him, and likely what all of the children called him when they were young. That one detail does a lot. It suggests a family vocabulary of warmth and closeness, even when William's public obligations made him formidable.

Another son, **Richard Sward Taylor**, opens his reminiscence by placing the whole family on the dairy farm near Provo, with **two sisters, four brothers, and fifty cows**. That is one of the best little windows in the surviving record. It tells us instantly that this was not a decorative household but a laboring one — children, stock, chores, and a father whose identity cannot be separated from the work-world around him.

### Mission to Australia

A major turning point in William Weldon's life was his call to the **Australian Mission**, where he served from **June 1912 to September 1914**, eventually as **mission president** from **1913 to 1914**.

This was not a romantic bachelor mission. He was a married man with a wife and children. The family material makes clear how much strain this placed on the home. The existing memoir-based biography says that when he was called, the household was already under pressure: little **Virginia** was ill, Nora was carrying the emotional and practical weight of the home, and yet William felt bound to go.

That combination — obedience, sacrifice, strain, and the suffering of the family left behind — feels central to understanding both William and Nora. It is easy to summarize such a mission as merely an item of church service. In reality it meant two years of separation, uncertainty, and the practical heroism of the woman who stayed home.

### Bishop Taylor

William was remembered as **Bishop Taylor**, and by the time of his death he was also serving on the **High Council of the Sharon Stake**. He was a church man in the fullest possible sense — not merely believing, but administratively responsible, publicly visible, and repeatedly called on.

His memorial text says that for the greater number of his fifty-four years, "his every act" showed how keenly adjusted he was to the wants and needs of others. That kind of sentence can sound exaggerated until you put it beside the children's recollections, which show a household permanently shaped by his service and absences.

One later summary line, preserved in the earlier biography, says that because of the demands of business and church, **he was unable to spend as much time as he would have loved to spend with his family, but he was very proud of and concerned for the welfare of each one**. That feels exactly right: not a cold patriarch, not an easy one, but a heavily obligated father whose love was felt by the children even through the strain.

## Nora Johnson Taylor

**Born:** 26 June 1886, Lakeview, Utah County, Utah Territory  
**Died:** 16 November 1935  
**Parents:** John Johnson and Inger Sward

If William's life was drawn outward, Nora's was the life that held the inside together.

The memoirs are remarkably consistent on this point. Her children remember her not in public offices or dramatic language, but in acts of constancy: care, sympathy, attention, nursing, understanding, and the emotional labor of making a very full family life feel habitable.

### Daughter of the Johnsons of Lakeview

Nora was born into one of the strongest Scandinavian immigrant families in the Lakeview area. Her father, **John Johnson**, came from Norway; her mother, **Inger Sward**, came from Sweden. Their family history — preserved by Jewel A. Bartholomew and by John's son August — is one of ocean passage, plains travel, settlement, farming, ward leadership, home industry, and large-family endurance.

That Johnson-Sward background matters for understanding Nora. She was not an isolated figure who entered the Taylor line without her own inherited world. She came out of a house where religion and labor were inseparable, where women made homes beautiful and habitable under hard circumstances, and where children were expected to work, read, improve themselves, and remain loyal to one another.

Her mother **Inger** in particular seems important to Nora's later shape. Inger is remembered as capable, artistic, determined, domestic, and spiritually serious. Nora appears in later memory as carrying that same inward steadiness into the Taylor household.

### What the children remembered of Nora

The best surviving line about Nora may be the one preserved from one of the children's recollections: **"Mother was always very understanding and helpful."** It is not ornate, but it has the ring of truth.

Another child, **Phillip Royden Taylor**, begins his section **"MEMORIES OF MOTHER"** with the line: **"As I remember Mother, she really lov..."** — the OCR trails off, but the emotional direction is obvious. The memoir packet as a whole repeatedly frames Nora through affection rather than authority.

The earlier family biography captured the essential point well: **"Nora filled in the gap with the children."** That is not a decorative sentence. It is the key to her life.

Where William was pulled outward by bishopric duties, mission calls, and public obligations, Nora absorbed the consequences at home. She was the one who kept seven children emotionally gathered, interpreted their father to them, nursed them through sickness, and made home a place of attachment rather than just discipline.

### Sweethearts always

One of the nicest little remembered stories in the material is not really a story at all but a verdict. A descendant note says that **Will and Nora were very much in love and completely devoted to one another**, and that they were known as sweethearts always.

That is exactly the sort of line family memory loves to preserve, and often enough it becomes bland with repetition. Here, though, it matters because it helps explain the emotional tone of the whole family recollection. The children do not remember a merely dutiful marriage. They remember real attachment.

That makes later events hit harder.

### Their children and the shape of the house

William and Nora had **seven children**:

- **Weldon Johnson Taylor**  
- **Virginia Taylor**  
- **Margaret Taylor**  
- **Phillip Royden Taylor**  
- **Richard Sward Taylor**  
- **Robert W. Taylor**  
- **Paul Hector Taylor**

The names themselves tell the story of family loyalties: Johnson, Sward, Taylor — old names carried forward into the next generation.

The house they built was large, busy, and work-centered. Richard's description of the Provo dairy farm with fifty cows is especially valuable because it gives the children not as sentimental faces around a table but as members of a work unit. The domestic scene was not just food and affection. It was labor, stock, routine, and the deep intimacy that comes from many people living under pressure.

That pressure likely made Nora even more central. In a household like that, the mother determines whether the family remembers childhood as secure or merely exhausting.

The surviving recollections suggest that Nora made it secure.

### Illness

The most painful part of Nora's biography is the one that her children never seem to have forgotten: her prolonged illness.

By the mid-1930s she had suffered enough that surgery is mentioned in the family materials, and the atmosphere of the home was darkened by the awareness that her health was failing. This comes to a head in **January 1935**, when son **Paul** left for the **Australian mission**.

The memoir-based account says that Nora was gravely ill at the time, and yet both she and William wanted him to go. The family memory emphasizes the emotional cost of that decision — not as abstract faithfulness, but as a real wrenching sacrifice.

The date matters because Nora died that same year, on **16 November 1935**.

The surviving family materials suggest that her illness and death left a wound that did not heal quickly in the household. In the children's memories, she is not merely one beloved parent among two. She is often the felt center of comfort and emotional coherence.

When she died, the structure of the family changed in a way that everyone seems to have understood immediately.

### Nora in the eyes of the family

The reason Nora deserves more than a supporting role in this biography is precisely that the sources remember her so strongly.

She is not public in the way William is public. There is no equivalent record of missions, council positions, bishopric titles, or obituary rhetoric about broad service. Instead, Nora's life survives in the voices of children who remember what it felt like to live with her.

That sort of evidence is easy to undervalue. It should not be.

When several children independently remember the same parent as loving, understanding, helpful, and central to the emotional life of the home, that is biography — not a lesser kind of biography, but one that often tells us more than the public record does.

## Their life together

William Weldon Taylor and Nora Johnson joined two strong Utah County lines: the T ay lor line of Provo and the Johnson-Sward line of Lakeview. The result was not simply a respectable Mormon household, but a highly textured one — full of labor, religion, affection, burden, sacrifice, and children who never quite stopped thinking about what their parents had been to them.

If one wanted the short version of their marriage, it might be this:
- **William bore the public burdens**  
- **Nora bore the private ones**

But that is too simple unless we add that the two were remembered as deeply devoted to one another. This was not a house split between ambition and duty. It was a house held together by both.

The surviving record suggests several defining themes in their life together:

### 1. A marriage of real attachment
They were remembered as sweethearts, not just as co-parents.

### 2. A home under pressure
Seven children, dairy-farm life, church demands, missions, and chronic illness made ease impossible.

### 3. Service and its cost
William's life of service was genuine, but it cost Nora and the children something real.

### 4. The children never forgot
What remains of them now lives not mainly in formal records, but in their children's memories: how they spoke, how they worked, how they loved, how they suffered, and what the house felt like.

That is exactly the sort of thing a family biography ought to preserve.

Their son **Paul Hector Taylor** carried their line forward. Through Paul, and then through **Byron William Taylor**, their life reaches Erik.
