## Clarence Jordan Naylor

**Born:** 14 August 1927, Safford, Graham County, Arizona  
**Died:** 25 May 1980, San Diego, California  
**Buried:** Greenwood Cemetery, San Diego, California

Clarence Jordan Naylor belonged to a later generation than most of the pioneer and immigrant figures in this family history. He was not a handcart traveler, homesteader, or Scandinavian convert. He was born into a fully established Mormon family world in the American West — a world where the old pioneer inheritance had already hardened into towns, institutions, missions, schools, and professional careers.

He was born in **Safford, Arizona**, the son of **Thomas C. Naylor** and **Caroline Layton Naylor**. The 1970 church article that announced his installation as president of the **San Diego North Stake** gives that parentage clearly and identifies him as a son of Thomas C. and Caroline Layton Naylor. By the time he reached adulthood, the family line had moved well beyond bare survival and settlement. In Clarence's life, the inherited virtues of Mormon frontier culture — discipline, family loyalty, religious commitment, and public service — show up in a different register: not fort building and irrigation ditches, but missions, church administration, federal law enforcement, and a famously large San Diego household.

### Mission and Early Adult Life

One of the family images attached to his record is captioned **"Jordan Naylor Mission photo 1949."** The 1970 church article adds the broader frame: he had served a mission in the **Eastern States**. Taken together, those sources make clear that missionary service was one of the foundational experiences of his young adulthood.

The mission matters because it places him squarely inside the classic 20th-century Latter-day Saint male track: raised in the church, sent into missionary work, then drawn deeper into both family life and church leadership afterward. The sources presently in hand do not give the detailed day-to-day mission narrative that some earlier ancestors have left behind, but the mission is still part of the shape of his life, not a passing footnote.

At some point after that period, he married **Marian Ward of Los Angeles**, as the *Deseret News* article phrases it. The exact date is not yet recovered in the current extraction set, but the wedding photographs — one small and one larger, both labeled **Wedding Day** — make the union visually present even where the documentary detail is still thin.

## Marian Rula Ward

Marian Rula Ward enters the surviving record much more quietly than Clarence does. Her GEDCOM record is sparse, and the strongest specific image source currently attached to her is a portrait captioned:

> **"Marian Rula Ward Naylor — Age 17"**

That image matters more than it might seem. It tells us that before she was Marian Naylor, mother in a San Diego household of twelve and wife of an FBI agent and stake president, she was remembered as Marian Ward — young, identifiable, and part of a family line that mattered enough for descendants to preserve.

From the broader family tree, she is the daughter of **Ernest Trulock Ward** and **Anna Elizabeth Barker**, and thus carries the Ward line that runs back into earlier Utah and English-American ancestry. The 1970 church article identifies her, simply but significantly, as **Marian Ward of Los Angeles** when she married Clarence.

The lack of dense individual documentation for Marian in the currently extracted set should not be mistaken for lack of importance. It just means that, in this particular source cluster, she appears more through household and relational records than through a standalone life sketch or obituary.

What we can say clearly is that she stood at the center of a very large family, participated with Clarence in an intensely active Latter-day Saint home, and was publicly visible enough that the family newspaper feature presented the Naylors not as Clarence's achievement alone but as the household of **Mr. and Mrs. C. Jordan Naylor**.

## FBI Agent, Bishop, Stake President

If Clarence's life has a public backbone, it is the combination of **professional law-enforcement work** and **serious church leadership**.

The *Deseret News* article from **14 November 1970** says that he had served for **20 years as a special agent for the Federal Bureau of Investigation**. The Family of the Year article, apparently from around the same period, says he had been an **FBI agent for more than 25 years**, **15 of them in San Diego**. Those figures do not line up perfectly, but they agree on the large point: Clarence Jordan Naylor spent decades in the FBI and built a major part of his public identity around disciplined federal service.

That same 1970 church article presents the parallel church track just as clearly. Before being installed as president of the **San Diego North Stake**, he had already served as:

- a **missionary in the Eastern States**
- **bishop's counselor in two wards**
- **bishop for three years**

Then, under the direction of **Ezra Taft Benson**, he was installed as stake president at the recent conference of the San Diego North Stake. The article names the counselors retained with him and positions him as the successor to the departing stake president.

This is a different kind of Mormon biography than the 19th-century material. Clarence was not plowing new land or fighting off crop failure. He was serving inside a mature institutional church — a man of responsibility, reliability, and public trust in both religious and civic life.

And the pairing is striking. On the one hand: FBI agent. On the other: bishop, counselor, stake president. Even without romanticizing it, that combination says something about how he was perceived — a man of order, discipline, trustworthiness, and command.

## A Household Large Enough To Become News

The richest single narrative source for Clarence and Marian is the **Family of the Year** article. It is exactly the sort of document a biography needs: not a certificate, not a bare obituary, but a contemporary piece of prose trying to describe a family as it appeared to outsiders.

The article begins with a touch of humor. One requirement for the San Diego **Family of the Year** recognition, sponsored by the San Diego District of the California Federation of Women's Clubs, was that the family should have at least one teenager. The article notes that the Naylor family had not just one but many teenagers — plus children who had already been teenagers, and still more coming behind them.

At the time of the article, the Naylors had **12 children** named in print:

- **Cindy**, 23, a student at Brigham Young University  
- **Scott Jordan**, 21, a missionary in Japan  
- **Sherri Lynn**, 19, a student at Brigham Young University  
- **Mark David**, 18, student at Dixie College  
- **Cathleen Ann**, 18, student at Dixie College  
- **Thomas Ward**, 16, student at Clairemont High School  
- **Carol Lynn**, 14  
- **Nancy Lee**, 12  
- **Joseph Terry**, 10  
- **Paul Layton**, 8  
- **Kenneth Ernest**, 4  
- **Christopher Jay**, 2

The article also says that Mrs. Naylor was expecting another child the following April, which fixes the family in a very particular moment: not a finished retrospective picture, but a household still actively growing.

That list is valuable not just for names and ages, but because it captures the Naylors at full scale. This was not a small or quiet family. It was a dense, kinetic household stretching from college students and missionaries down to toddlers.

### The Adoption Story

The most moving detail in the Family of the Year article is not the award itself but the explanation that **three of the 12 children were adopted** after being orphaned.

The article says their mother had been **Mrs. Naylor's sister**, and their father had been **Mr. Naylor's brother**. In other words, these were not abstract adoptions but children doubly tied by blood to the Naylor household. When they were orphaned, they were taken into Clarence and Marian's home in **University City**.

That detail transforms the family portrait. The large household was not simply the result of fertility, enthusiasm, or a cheerful taste for chaos. It was also the result of kinship obligation accepted at full cost. Clarence and Marian did not merely sympathize with tragedy in the extended family. They took it into their own house.

That single fact also helps explain why the family became a public symbol in the article. The household was being recognized not only for size, but for the visible exercise of virtues the article praises: lawfulness, honesty, industry, church devotion, love, and mutual respect.

Whatever one thinks of awards like Family of the Year, the article gives us something useful and real: a contemporary outsider's view of the Naylors as a household large enough, disciplined enough, and generous enough to attract public attention.

## Clarence and Marian Together

The danger with a source set like this is that Clarence can become all office and role, while Marian risks becoming just "the wife." But the Family of the Year article quietly resists that. It is explicitly about **Mr. and Mrs. C. Jordan Naylor**. The adopted children are explained through Marian's kin as well as Jordan's. The article presents the home as a joint moral project, not simply a patriarch's résumé.

So although Clarence is the more documented public figure in the current record, the household story itself makes clear that Marian must be understood as a co-builder of the life being described. A man can be an FBI agent and stake president, but he cannot be one half of a twelve-child home — especially one expanded by taking in orphaned relatives — without a wife equally central to the family structure.

The surviving sources do not yet give Marian an extended independent voice. They do give her place, which is not the same thing as absence. She appears as:

- **Marian Ward of Los Angeles** in the marriage frame,
- **Marian Rula Ward Naylor** in youth,
- wife beside Clarence in wedding images,
- co-parent in the Family of the Year feature,
- sister to the mother of the orphaned children they adopted,
- and mother of **Cynthia Ann Naylor**, Erik's mother.

That is already enough to show that she was not incidental to the story being told.

## Public Respectability and Family Density

There is something almost quintessentially mid-century Mormon-American about the form Clarence and Marian's life took.

They were not remembered through a single dramatic pioneer incident, but through a durable pattern:
- mission
- marriage
- large family
- church office
- federal service
- educational ambition for children
- kinship care
- public respectability

The children in the article are in BYU, Dixie College, Japanese mission service, elementary school, junior high, high school. Clarence is in the FBI. The family is in San Diego, but still deeply tied to the institutional world of the Latter-day Saints. The whole picture feels like a later phase of the same tradition that shaped the older Utah ancestors — now translated into suburban California, church administration, and professional life.

The church article and the Family of the Year feature reinforce each other nicely here. One shows Clarence as a trusted leader in the church. The other shows the Naylors as a trusted family in civic and social life. The overlap is the point.

## Death and Legacy

Clarence Jordan Naylor died on **25 May 1980** in **San Diego**, and was buried at **Greenwood Cemetery**. The current source set does not yet include a full obituary, which means the late-life section of this biography is necessarily thinner than the middle. But even from the available record, his legacy is clear enough.

He left behind a family line that reaches directly to Erik through **Cynthia Ann Naylor**, and he also left the kind of reputation that survives in scattered but telling records: church office, federal service, public recognition, and family photographs preserved because later generations still wanted to look back at him.

Marian's fuller late-life story remains to be fleshed out from additional records. But her place in the line is secure, and her importance in the household described here is unmistakable.
