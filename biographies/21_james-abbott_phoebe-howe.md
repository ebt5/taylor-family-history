# James Abbott & Phoebe Howe
## Biography #21 — The Wyoming Massacre Captive

**Relationship to Erik Taylor:** 7× great-grandparents through the Abbott line. This family represents one of the most dramatic stories in Erik's heritage — a Revolutionary War captain, an Indian captivity during the Wyoming Massacre, and a line that winds through Nauvoo and into the early LDS church.

Line: Erik → Cynthia Naylor → Clarence Jordan Naylor → Phoebe Caroline Layton → Cynthia Abigail Fife → Phoebe Abigail Abbott → **James Abbott & Phoebe Howe**

## Prologue: Two Lives Shaped by Revolution and Frontier Violence

James Abbott and Phoebe Howe lived through the American Revolution and its aftermath — a period of extraordinary violence, displacement, and transformation. Their lives intersected at one of the most harrowing moments of the Revolutionary War: the **Wyoming Massacre of 3 July 1778**, a Loyalist and Iroquois attack on the Wyoming Valley of Pennsylvania that killed hundreds of settlers and sent shockwaves through the entire frontier.

Of Phoebe Howe, the family tradition records a story so remarkable that it has been passed down through seven generations to reach Erik Taylor's line.

---

## The Wyoming Massacre, July 3, 1778

The Wyoming Valley — a stretch of fertile land along the Susquehanna River in what is now northeastern Pennsylvania — was, by the summer of 1778, a contested frontier. The Revolutionary War had brought British-allied Loyalists and their Iroquois confederates against the patriot settlers who had carved farms from the wilderness.

On **3 July 1778**, a force of Loyalists and Iroquois warriors attacked the Wyoming Valley settlement. The resulting battle and massacre killed approximately 300 American patriots. Farms were burned. Women and children were captured. The event became known as the **Wyoming Massacre** — one of the most traumatic episodes of the Revolutionary War on the Pennsylvania frontier.

**Phoebe Howe was among those captured.**

She was fifteen years old.

---

## Phoebe's Captivity: Saved by Her Beauty

The family biography, recorded in *"Mary Luella Abbott Leavitt 1865-1955"* by Bruce McIntire, preserves the tradition with startling directness:

> *"Family tradition states that Phebe was one of those captured by the Indians during a battle, but because of her beauty, she was not killed and she was later ransomed."*

The note in the family GEDCOM elaborates:

> *"Phoebe Howe Coray was a participant in the famous Wyoming Massacre, and was carried with [other captives]..."*

Phoebe was taken, held captive, and then ransomed — returned to her family and community. The experience left no documented injury to her body, but it shaped her identity for the rest of her long life. She was a survivor of one of the frontier's most brutal events, spared by the chance of her youth and beauty in a moment of terrible violence.

Her father was likely the **Mr. Howe who later purchased a mill from James Abbott** — a connection that suggests the two families knew each other, perhaps before Phoebe was even born, and certainly in the years that followed the Massacre.

---

## James Abbott: Revolutionary War Captain

While Phoebe was surviving captivity, James Abbott was fighting the Revolution.

James Abbott was born 9 March 1753 in Windham, Connecticut. His father, **Abiel Abbott**, was a grantee in the **Susquehanna Purchase of 1754** — a land claim that placed the Abbott family squarely in the contested Wyoming Valley region. Philip, James's older brother, inherited the Susquehanna lots, but conveyed them to James in 1777 (Philip held them until September 23, 1777, then gave them to James, who eventually sold them in June 1787 to Matthias Hollenback).

James Abbott's military record is documented:

> **Veteran Revolutionary War**  
> **SGT, CAPT Clark's Co., COL Sage's Regt, Conn Line**

He served as a Sergeant and rose to Captain. He was in Connecticut Line units — part of the Continental Army's organized regiments. A Presidential Certificate signed by President Obama (presumably for Revolutionary War veterans' descendants) honoring James Abbott's military service is preserved in family documents. His tombstone bears witness to his patriotism:

> *"To the Memory of James Abbott, one of the patriots of 1776, who died May 2, 1830. Aged 77 years."*

James came from the Wyoming Valley to the Canisteo Valley in New York, settling near **Arkport** in Steuben County around **1806**. It was in this new frontier that James and Phoebe built their life together.

---

## Phoebe's First Marriage: John Coray

Before James, Phoebe had lived another life entirely.

After being ransomed from captivity, Phoebe Howe grew up in the Wyoming Valley. Around **1786** she met and married **John Coray** in the Hornellsville, Steuben, Pennsylvania area. They had four sons together. Then, around **1797**, tragedy struck: **John Coray was accidentally shot by one of his neighbors**. Phoebe was widowed with four young sons.

She was approximately 34 years old.

---

## James and Phoebe: A Second Beginning

On **17 January 1798**, at Kingston, Luzerne, Pennsylvania, Phoebe Howe Coray married **James Abbott**. She brought her four Coray sons; James brought his history as a Continental soldier and Wyoming Valley patriarch. Together, in the years that followed, they moved to Allegany County, New York, near Arkport.

Five children were born to James and Phoebe:
1. James Abbott Jr.
2. [three others]
3. **Stephen Joseph Abbott** (born 16 August 1804, Providence, Pennsylvania) — the 4th son; the ancestor of Erik's line

James and Phoebe operated **a linen weaving mill** — James Abbott was a miller and weaver in his later years, combining the industrial skills of the era with the frontier self-sufficiency of the Steuben County settlements.

The marriage eventually ended. The family records note they *"later divorced after 1813."* The reasons are not documented. But when Phoebe died in 1842, a monument was placed *"next to her husband James Abbott in the cemetery at Arkport"* — suggesting a posthumous reconciliation of memory, if not of life.

---

## Stephen Joseph Abbott: The Link to LDS History

The fourth son of James and Phoebe, **Stephen Joseph Abbott**, was born 16 August 1804 in Providence, Pennsylvania. On 11 December 1825, he married **Abigail Smith** in Dansville, Steuben, New York.

In **1839**, Stephen and Abigail — then living in Illinois near Nauvoo — came into contact with the early Latter-day Saint community. *"Stephen and his wife Abigail came in contact with the Mormon people, who on being driven out of Missouri were settling in Nauvoo, Illinois."* They were baptized and joined the Church.

Stephen Joseph Abbott and Abigail Smith's daughter **Phoebe Abigail Abbott** (named for her grandmother, the captive) married **William Fife** — and that union became Biography #12 in this series. Through the Fife–Abbott–Layton–Naylor line, the story of the Wyoming Massacre survivor winds its way across seven generations to Cynthia Ann Naylor and her son Erik Taylor.

---

## Phoebe's Final Journey: Illinois and the Early Saints

In 1838, at age 75, Phoebe Howe Coray Abbott made one more journey. She traveled from New York to **Illinois**, going to live near her son James Abbott Jr. and his family — and near her son Stephen Joseph Abbott and his family in what would become the Nauvoo area.

*"Again Phoebe was surrounded by her family and friends."* In 1839, as Stephen and Abigail joined the Saints, the elderly Phoebe witnessed the early days of Nauvoo. She was in her late seventies as the LDS movement took shape around her.

**Phoebe Howe died 9 September 1842**, approximately twelve miles from Naples, Pike County, Illinois. She was **79 years old**. She had been born a colonial subject of the British Crown, survived an Indian captivity in the Revolutionary War, outlived two husbands, and died as an old woman surrounded by her pioneer-faith family in Illinois — a year before the martyrdom of Joseph Smith.

A biography compiled by Lucy Brown Archer begins: *"Phoebe Howe Coray Abbott — Born: 21 February 1763 at Kingston, Luzerne, Pennsylvania. Died: 9 September 1842 twelve miles from Naples, Pike, Illinois."*

---

## James Abbott's Grave

James Abbott died 2 May 1830 in Hornellsville, Steuben, New York. He was 77 years old.

His grave stands in a small private plot on the **Merry Brothers Farm in Arkport, New York** — in the middle of a cornfield, with a fence around it. About eleven graves are located there according to the Arkport town historian. His headstone reads:

> *"To the Memory of James Abbott, one of the patriots of 1776, who died May 2, 1830. Aged 77 years."*

The grave was described in *An Historical Sketch of Arkport* by William Hurlbut, published in the *Canisteo Valley Times* in 1876 — forty-six years after James's death, when local memory was still alive with his story.

---

## What Phoebe's Story Means

Phoebe Howe's captivity during the Wyoming Massacre is one of the few truly documented dramatic events in Erik Taylor's deep ancestry. The tradition is preserved in multiple independent family sources — the biography by Bruce McIntire, the family GEDCOM notes, the genealogy of George and Hannah Abbott. The consistency of the story across sources lends it credibility.

She was fifteen, captured in a raid that killed hundreds of her neighbors, spared because her captors found her beautiful, and ransomed back to her community. She married twice, bore nine children (four Coray, five Abbott), ran a linen mill, and died at 79 surrounded by the Mormon pioneers her son had joined.

The Abbott family history — compiled by John Austin Abbott and edited by Mary Lou (Abbott) Shaw in 1993 — preserves this lineage in the genealogy of *George and Hannah Abbott and Their Descendants*.

Erik Taylor carries in his heritage the blood of a fifteen-year-old girl who survived the Wyoming Massacre by the grace of her beauty — and lived to see her great-great-great-grandchildren embracing the faith of the Restoration.
