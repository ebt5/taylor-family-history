## William Joseph Taylor Jr.

**Born:** 23 November 1859, Provo, Utah Territory  
**Died:** 30 December 1936, Provo / Lakeview area, Utah  
**Buried:** 3 January 1937, Provo City Cemetery  
**Mission:** Southern States Mission, 1888

William Joseph Taylor Jr. was born in Provo six years after his parents arrived in Utah. He was the son of **William Joseph Taylor Sr.** and **Mary Bowring**, and he grew up in one of the old Mormon pioneer families of Utah County. His father belonged to the English convert generation and is remembered in family sources for planting the well-known Provo oak sometimes described in family memory as the "Pioneer Tree," grown from an acorn carried west.

William Jr. spent his early life in Provo. By 1880 he appears in the census there as a **farmer**, which fits the pattern of his later life: practical work, public service, and family labor rather than any kind of fame or office that would have made him widely known beyond his community.

One family source says that after the birth of the third child, **Scott Andrew**, the family moved back to Provo because William Joseph had been called on a mission to the **Southern States** for The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. The GEDCOM likewise records a mission in **1888**. The sources presently in hand do not let me reconstruct the whole mission in detail, but they do make clear that the mission mattered enough to be remembered in later family history, especially because of the hardship it imposed on Abby while he was gone.

After his return from that mission, William's work life becomes easier to trace. According to the scanned family history page, he:

- worked for **William Probert** helping build a street railway from Provo to Utah Lake,
- worked as an **assistant mechanic at the Woolen Mills**,
- served as **Provo City Watermaster** from **1892 to 1895**,
- served as **Provo Fire Chief** from **1896 to 1899**,
- served as **Sexton** from **1900 to 1903**, and
- sang for many years in both the **Provo Tabernacle Choir** and the **Second Ward Choir**.

That list is one of the best snapshots of his character. He was not simply a farmer in the narrow sense. He was the kind of town-and-ward man who took whatever practical responsibilities needed doing: water, fire protection, cemetery work, mechanical labor, church music, and then, later, large-scale family farming.

### Move to Vineyard and Lakeview

Around **1904**, William decided he needed a change. The family history explains the reason in very plain terms: he had a houseful of sons, and he needed a way for them to work and gain independence. A farm seemed the best answer. He first bought the **Amos D. Holdaway ranch in Vineyard**, but the mud and isolation made that location impractical before the era of paved roads. After about a year there, he purchased the **J. D. Dilatush / George C. Scott farm in Lakeview**.

This move seems to have been one of the decisive turns in the family's life. From there he expanded, later adding the land of **Frank Hopkins** on the north and **Hyrum Scott** on the south. With his sons he established **Cherry Hill Dairy** in **1906**. The dairy took its name from the orchard of cherry trees stretching south from the family home.

In the *Lakeview History*, Paul H. Taylor's account of Cherry Hill Farm remembers this place not as an abstraction but as a working landscape of orchards, barns, teams, lumber hauling, and fruit trees: cherries, quince, plums, apples, peaches, and apricots. William and Abby were trying to build exactly the sort of large-family, labor-sharing farm economy that could absorb sons growing into adulthood.

### A family man with public usefulness

What stands out from the sources is that William Jr. was not remembered through one dramatic episode, but through steady usefulness. He was a husband, father, civic worker, choir singer, and farmer. The scanned page from the family story describes his decision-making in explicitly paternal terms: he saw the need for his sons to have something to do and some way to grow independent. That is the sort of detail that feels true to life because it is so practical.

He and Abby raised a large family through years that included mission separations, illness, hard physical work, and repeated moves. Their children were born in Provo, and later the family identity became strongly tied to Lakeview and Cherry Hill.

William survived Abby by nearly six years. He died on **30 December 1936**, and was buried in Provo City Cemetery on **3 January 1937**.

## Abby Jane Scott Taylor

**Born:** 30 January 1864, Provo, Utah Territory  
**Died:** 27 February 1931, Provo, Utah  
**Buried:** 1 March 1931, Provo City Cemetery

Abby Jane Scott Taylor comes through the sources with more personal texture than William does, largely because one of her daughters, **Mary Taylor Dahlquist**, wrote a life sketch about her.

That life sketch begins in high pioneer style but then quickly becomes concrete. Abby was the **eighth of eleven children** born to **Andrew Hunter Scott** and **Sarah Ann Roe Scott**, one of the substantial early families of Provo. Her daughter presents her as a representative Latter-day Saint woman whose real greatness lay not in public distinction but in wifehood, motherhood, sacrifice, and endurance.

The list of her siblings, preserved in the life sketch, places her in a large and busy family network:

1. Franklin  
2. Walter  
3. Anna  
4. Andrew Hunter  
5. Mary Emma  
6. Camby  
7. Sarah Ann  
8. **Abby Jane**  
9. Howard  
10. Roselle Eugene  
11. Francelle

### Childhood in Provo orchards

Abby was born on the Scott farm in Provo. Her daughter remembered the place as a large tract planted heavily in fruit trees, especially apples, and described Andrew Hunter Scott as an early nurseryman who brought nursery stock and seeds west from Council Bluffs. Whether every family memory can be pinned down to documentary proof, the picture itself is vivid and credible: Abby grew up in a fruit-growing household where harvest, drying fruit, and helping supply markets were ordinary parts of life.

The life sketch remembers the neighborhood labor of that world — drying apples and peaches, handling nursery stock, and helping one another in practical ways. It also preserves small family stories, like the mischievous switching of labels on fruit trees so a customer would carry off crooked saplings instead of the straight ones he had selected. Those are the kinds of details that make the Scott home feel inhabited rather than merely genealogical.

### Baptism and youth

According to the life sketch, Abby was baptized on **3 September 1875** by **Albert Jones** and confirmed by **George Thatcher**, in the Main Ditch stream on 5th West in Provo. That detail gives a nice glimpse of early Utah Mormon life in which canals and streams served both practical and religious uses.

After her father's death, Abby spent time living with siblings and helping them with their families. She was expected to work young. One of the most striking details in her daughter's account is that at about **fifteen years old** Abby cooked for **railroad construction crews**, first on the Denver and Rio Grande narrow gauge line through Parleys Canyon toward Park City, then on another contract connected with her brother Andrew. This was not sheltered life. It was domestic labor performed in frontier-industrial conditions.

Later she worked in the household of **Zina Young Williams**, a daughter of Brigham Young, and there became acquainted with related church family circles in Salt Lake. Whether that employment was long or short, it suggests that by young womanhood Abby was already known as competent, trustworthy, and capable in domestic management.

### Marriage to William Joseph Taylor

Abby married **William Joseph Taylor** on **3 August 1882** in the **Endowment House** in Salt Lake City. Her daughter says the ceremony was performed by **Daniel H. Wells**.

The newspaper obituary confirms the marriage date and place, and also says that Abby was the daughter of **Bishop Andrew H. Scott** and **Sarah Humphries Scott**. It gives her birth date as **January 4, 1864**. The death certificate gives her mother's name as **Sarah Ann Humphries**, while the broader family records identify her mother as **Sarah Ann Roe**; this is a discrepancy worth preserving rather than smoothing over, since family records often contain overlapping surnames or mistaken maternal identifications.

The Taylors first lived in **Provo**, where their early children were born. The family record image gives a clear list of the household Abby and William built.

### Children

The family record currently visible gives these children of William Joseph Taylor and Abby Jane Scott:

1. **William Weldon Taylor**, b. 9 Dec 1883  
2. **Mary Taylor**, b. 8 Oct 1885  
3. **Andrew Scott Taylor**, b. 11 Apr 1887  
4. **Joseph Hyrum Taylor**, b. 27 Jan 1889  
5. **Ruth Elva Taylor**, b. 13 Dec 1891  
6. **Golden Taylor**, b. 15 Jan 1893  
7. **Frank Rouzelle Taylor**, b. 8 Mar 1895  
8. **Glen Lee Taylor**, b. 24 Jul 1897  
9. **Della Scott Taylor**, b. 8 Nov 1899  
10. **Ruby Taylor**, b. 4 Jan 1902  
11. **Karl Roe Taylor**, b. 4 Dec 1904

The obituary, however, indicates there were **eleven surviving children** in 1931 and names them as William W., Scott, Ruth Johnson, Della Jensen, Mary Dahlquist, Joseph H., Golden, Glen, Ruby Olsen, Frank R., and Karl — eleven names. That means the family was generally remembered as having **eleven children**, not twelve. Earlier claims of twelve or thirteen should therefore be treated with caution until checked against the full compilation and broader records.

One of these children was **Paul Hector Taylor**, Erik's grandfather, but he is not visible in the currently extracted family record image. That suggests the screenshot/transcription I could recover is incomplete or cropped, not that Paul Hector was absent from the family.

### Hard years while William was on mission

The strongest section of Abby's life sketch is the account of what happened while William served in the Southern States Mission.

Mary Dahlquist says that Abby endured severe hardships while her husband was away. She chopped wood for fuel, worked where she could, and somehow fed a growing family with several small children. One child was born shortly after William left. Another, **Scott**, became dangerously ill as a toddler. Abby and family members nursed him carefully; the memoir emphasizes how close to disaster it felt. Later **William Weldon** came down with typhoid fever and again the family feared losing him.

This is the core of Abby's biography: less public than William, but perhaps more heroic in private terms. The sources do not present her as ornamental or merely pious. They show her laboring physically, managing illness, and carrying the household through crisis.

### Relief Society and community standing

The obituary says Abby was a faithful member of the Latter-day Saint church and had taken an active part in **Primary** and **Relief Society** work, at one time serving as a **counselor in the Relief Society of the Lake View Ward**. That is backed up by the tone of both the life sketch and funeral article, which treat her as a known and respected church woman.

The funeral report says the **Lake View chapel was filled to capacity** on the Sunday after her death. The speakers — **L. John Nuttall, Chris Jepperson, and President S. P. Eggertsen** — all dwelt on her "exemplary life and character." That sort of language is standard funeral language, but the full chapel and the roster of speakers suggest she really was widely esteemed.

### The move to Lakeview and Cherry Hill

In the spring of **1905**, according to the obituary, the family moved from Provo to **Lake View**, where they remained thereafter. This lines up neatly with the Cherry Hill Farm material, which places the family first in Vineyard and then on the Scott/Dilatush farm in Lakeview around that same period.

The move seems to have created the setting in which Abby spent the second half of her life: not town-centered Provo, but a large farm-centered household in Lakeview, with orchards, dairy work, and children growing into adults around her. The *Lakeview History* excerpt by Paul H. Taylor strongly reinforces that the farm became central to family identity.

### Illness and death

Abby died on **27 February 1931** after what the obituary calls a **lingering illness**. The death certificate gives the underlying cause as **senile psychosis** of about **two years' duration**, with **influenza** for **seven days** as a contributory cause. She died at **9:15 a.m.** in Provo, though her usual residence remained **Lake View**. The certificate records her age as **67 years and 27 days**.

The obituary says she left:
- her husband, **W. J. Taylor**;
- sons and daughters including **William W., Scott, Joseph H., Golden, Glen, Frank R., Karl, Mary, Della, Ruth, and Ruby**;
- **44 grandchildren**;
- one brother, **Howard Scott of Provo**; and
- two sisters, **Mrs. Jesse Harding of Vineyard** and **Mrs. Walter Sorenson of Provo**.

Her funeral was held in the **Lake View chapel**, with music including **"A Perfect Day," "Going Home," "Just Beyond the Vail of Years,"** and **"O My Father."** She was buried in **Provo City Cemetery** on **1 March 1931**.

## Their life together

Taken together, William and Abby's story is the story of a solid Utah family in the generation after the first pioneers. They were not the wagon-generation converts themselves, but the children of that generation, born into settlements their parents had only recently built.

They married young, began in Provo, endured the missionary absences and illnesses that marked so many Latter-day Saint homes, then shifted toward a larger, work-centered family economy in Vineyard and Lakeview. William moved through a sequence of roles — farmer, mechanic, watermaster, fire chief, sexton, choir singer, dairyman. Abby held together the interior world of the household while also serving in church organizations and community life.

The most memorable image from their shared story is probably **Cherry Hill Dairy**: sons old enough to work, orchards stretching south from the home, a dairy run by the family, and the deliberate attempt to make land support children becoming adults. It is a practical, unsentimental family vision, and it seems to have defined their later years.

If Abby's life sketch gives the emotional center of the marriage, William's public work gives the outer framework. Between them, they represent a kind of second-generation Mormon domestic competence: neither famous nor obscure, but the sort of couple out of whom a region is actually made.

## Source notes and cautions

A few points remain unresolved and should be checked against the full *Joe and Abby* compilation before treating them as settled:

- Some family memories and later notes claim **twelve or thirteen children**, while the currently visible family record and obituary support **eleven** surviving named children.
- The currently extracted family record image does not visibly include all of the descendants one would expect to see in the wider Taylor line, which suggests the image or current extraction is incomplete.
- The obituary gives Abby's birth date as **4 January 1864**, while the life sketch and death certificate give **30 January 1864**. The death certificate and family life sketch agree, so I have treated **30 January 1864** as the better-supported date.
- The mother's surname appears in the broader family line as **Roe**, while Abby's death certificate reports **Sarah Ann Humphries**. This should be reconciled carefully from the Scott family records rather than guessed at.
