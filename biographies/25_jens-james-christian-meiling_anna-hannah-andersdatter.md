## Jens James Christian Pedersen Meiling

**Born:** 17 April 1834, Åsted (Aasted), Hjørring, Denmark  
**Christened:** 19 May 1834, Åsted, Hjørring, Denmark  
**Died:** 1 October 1902, Mount Pleasant, Sanpete, Utah (date supported by compiled biography and consistent with burial timing; newspaper notice confirms early October 1902 death)  
**Buried:** 3 October 1902, Mount Pleasant, Sanpete, Utah

Jens appears in records under a baffling number of names: **Jens Christian Pedersen**, **Jens Christian Christiansen**, **James Christian Meiling**, **James Meiling**, and several mixed forms in between. That alone is a useful warning about Scandinavian patronymics and Americanized surnames. The compiled family sketch explains that he later used **Meiling**, apparently derived from the place-name associated with his Danish home, in part to distinguish himself from the many other men named Jens Christiansen.

He was the only son of **Peder Christensen** and **Ane Kathrine Jensdatter**, born into a Danish household in the far north of Jutland. The family records preserved in the life sketch place him in the household censuses of 1834, 1840, 1845, and 1850, alongside his sisters Karen Marie, Johanne Kirstine, and Inger Marie. In a life that would later stretch from Denmark to Sanpete County, those census traces are the first small pieces of him we can see.

### Conversion and Emigration

According to the compiled biography, Jens was baptized in Denmark on **25 September 1853** by **Andrew Peterson**, and his older sister Karen followed into the faith a few years later. The sketch says only Jens and Karen among the immediate family joined the Mormon church and emigrated.

The emigration story is one of the places where the evidence becomes both rich and messy.

The **compiled life sketch** says Jens's journey to America began in **Copenhagen on 18 April 1857**, when he left on the steamer **L. N. Hvidt** with a company of Scandinavian Saints under **Hector C. Haight**. It goes on to place him in the 1857 Scandinavian migration stream through **Grimsby**, then **Liverpool**, and onto the ship **Westmoreland**, with **Matthias Cowley** presiding over the emigrating company. The biography preserves vivid company-level details: seasickness in the North Sea, passengers singing as they left Denmark, apostles visiting the emigrants before departure, ward organization and prayers on shipboard, water rationing, deck meetings, and the long slow pull into the Delaware River and Philadelphia.

But the **GEDCOM notes** preserve a different documentary trail: references to **Jens Christian Pedersen**, aged 21, traveling alone on the ship **John J. Boyd** in **1855-56**, and notes tied to that vessel's passenger logbook. Meanwhile, the **1902 newspaper notice** says he "came to Utah in **1856**."

So the clean version of the story — one ship, one year, one route — cannot yet be honestly told. The most responsible summary is this: Jens emigrated in the mid-1850s as part of the Scandinavian Mormon movement; there is strong family-biography evidence for a major 1857 company journey, but there are also documentary notes and a newspaper tradition pointing toward 1855-56. Until those are more fully reconciled, the discrepancy should remain visible.

### Across the Plains and Into Sanpete

Whatever the exact migration sequence, Jens did what thousands of Scandinavian converts did in those years: crossed an ocean, crossed the American interior, and ended up in Utah under church direction.

The compiled biography says he first lived briefly in **Ephraim**, then in **February 1859** moved north with about twenty men to help establish the settlement that became **Mount Pleasant**. He was one of the nine men who signed the petition to **Brigham Young** asking permission to create the new town. In that telling, Jens is not just a resident of Mount Pleasant after the fact — he is one of its actual founders.

The sketch of those first months is vivid. The men camped in a ravine, cut posts, moved onto the fort site in March, went back to Ephraim for families and supplies, and lived in tents and wagons while clearing heavy sagebrush and breaking ground. The biography notes that the burned ruins of the earlier settlement of Hambleton were still visible, and the fear of Indian attack never felt far away.

A surviving town record from May 1859 placed him in a structured role during the fort-building effort: **James C. Meiling**, captain of one of the work groups responsible for the fort line. The family biography makes a point of noting that his name was later omitted from a 1909 pioneer monument, apparently because of later tensions with the LDS Church and the mechanics of getting names placed there. Whether or not every detail of that explanation is exact, it reflects a family memory that Jens's later religious estrangement obscured his very real role in the founding generation of Mount Pleasant.

### Marriage Before Hannah

Before his long marriage to Hannah, Jens had an earlier marriage. The compiled biography says he married **Elizabeth Mette Clemensen** — likely in or around **1857**, linked to the emigrant period and Keokuk, Iowa. That marriage produced two sons, **Peter** and **Christian**.

The later family narrative says the marriage did not last and ended by mutual agreement after settlement in Utah. The surviving story says Elizabeth retained one son while Jens kept the other. Because these details come through later compiled family history rather than extracted primary records in this case file, they should be treated with care. What is clear is that when Jens married Hannah in 1863, he was not entering his first marriage, and the blended-family dimension matters to understanding the household they later built.

### Marriage to Hannah

Hannah's obituary says she was married in **1863** to Mr. Meiling. The compiled Hannah biography is more specific: **10 November 1863**, when Hannah was eighteen and Jens about twenty-nine. That date fits the rest of the family chronology well enough to treat it as probably correct.

Their marriage produced the branch of the family that would run directly down to Erik through **Veda Meiling**.

### Black Hawk War Era and Public Work

The GEDCOM records list military entries for Jens in **1866** and **1867** in Sanpete and Sevier. The compiled biography places him in the years of local defense and guard duty during the same broad Black Hawk War era. The exact service record remains to be pinned down more precisely, but the broad picture is clear: Jens was part of the generation of Sanpete settlers who farmed and built while also serving guard duty under the persistent threat of violence.

The family sketch portrays him as one of those broadly capable pioneer men who could farm, build, improvise, and organize. By later memory he was:

- a **farmer**,
- a **brickmaker**,
- a **carpenter/builder**,
- a **beekeeper**,
- a **Watermaster** for a period,
- a **Roadmaster** for one term, and
- a stockholder in the **Birch Creek Irrigation Company**.

Those roles fit the sort of local competence Sanpete settlements depended on. He was not merely working his own plot; he was one of the men who helped make the material life of the settlement possible.

### Colorado Colonization and Religious Estrangement

One of the most consequential episodes in the family story came in the **mid-1880s**, when Jens was called into a Colorado colonization effort. The family biography says he sold his farm, moved temporarily, and became deeply disillusioned by dishonest dealings among some of the colonists. The account also folds this into the larger and more painful story of his eventual break with the LDS Church.

The descendant narrative says that after these experiences, and after further pressures involving plural-marriage expectations and people he had trusted, Jens asked to have his name removed from the church records in **April 1885**. Hannah followed in **March 1886**. The same family account says that both later regretted this voluntary estrangement.

This section of the story needs careful handling. It is clearly important in family memory, and it likely explains some later oddities — including monument omission, Presbyterian funeral context, and a long aftertaste of sadness in descendant recollection. But the details come through compiled family narrative, not a packet of direct primary correspondence in the present case file. So the event should be preserved, but not melodramatized.

### Birch Creek, Cherrying Labor Into Land

After returning from Colorado, Jens bought the **Davidson/Birch Creek farm** north of Mount Pleasant — about seventy acres, according to the family history — and built a new frame house there around **1889**. By then he and Hannah had a large household of children. Their daughter **Veda** was born on **31 August 1888**, when Jens was fifty-four and Hannah forty-three.

The Birch Creek years appear to have been the mature center of their family life. The children attended school there at least irregularly, worked the land, helped with stock, and grew up in a house remembered for music, hard work, and family cohesion. Jens is remembered as a man who did his own carpentry and timber work, made and burned brick, tended his land carefully, and built not just a farm but a settled place.

A family tribute preserved in the compiled biography, attributed to his cousin **C. W. Sorenson**, gives the strongest surviving character sketch of him:

> "James Meiling was erect and rather handsome, a man of good habits, clean in personality and speech. I never heard an unkind word, a vulgar word, or a profane word fall from his lips. He was handy with tools, tackled almost anything, did well and finished what he undertook in a mechanical way. He made and burned his own brick, hewed and shaped his own timber, did his own carpentry for his home as well as his outbuildings on the farm, tended his own garden and made his home beautiful. He was a good neighbor, a good citizen, and a friendly and peaceable man..."

This is affectionate family testimony, not courtroom evidence. But it is so specific in its praise — brick, timber, carpentry, speech, garden, neighborliness — that it feels rooted in real observation rather than generic piety.

### Illness and Death

By **1900**, according to the family narrative, Jens knew he could not keep up the full strain of farm work much longer. He sold the Birch Creek farm and moved the family back into town because his health was failing. He took cold in **November 1901**, never fully recovered, and died on **1 October 1902**. The compiled family sketch adds one final detail of real significance: as death approached, **he asked to be rebaptized, but was physically unable to undergo the ordinance before he died**. That detail comes from the later family biography rather than the newspaper notice, but it fits the larger family memory that he regretted the estrangement from the church that had followed the Colorado episode.

The **1902 newspaper notice** is concise and valuable. It calls him:

> "one of the pioneer settlers of Mt. Pleasant and a well-known citizen of Sanpete county"

and says that he was:

> "prosperous and was respected as a man of sterling worth and integrity."

That contemporary wording matters. Even if later family histories had vanished, the newspaper would still tell us that by the time of his death he was remembered publicly as a successful, respected pioneer of real standing.

He was buried in **Mount Pleasant Cemetery** on **3 October 1902**.

## Anna Hannah Andersdatter

**Born:** 11 September 1845, Flade, Hjørring, Denmark  
**Christened:** 20 October 1845, Flade, Hjørring, Denmark  
**Died:** 24 April 1930, Medford, Jackson County, Oregon  
**Buried:** 28 April 1930, Mount Pleasant, Sanpete, Utah

Anna Hannah Andersdatter appears in family and American sources under many forms as well: **Anna**, **Hannah**, **Hanah**, **Pedersen**, **Peterson**, **Petersen**. In the Utah and descendant record she becomes most recognizably **Hannah Meiling**.

If Jens's story is one of building, Hannah's is one of survival.

### Emigration and Childhood Loss

The Hannah biography preserves a darker and more intimate migration story than the Jens sketch. Her family — **Anders Pedersen** and **Rasmine Olesdatter** — left Denmark in the mid-1850s with several children. The compiled account ties them to the ship **James Nesmith**, to winter storms, miserable shipboard conditions, river delays, Mormon Grove, and the overland trail under severe hardship.

The evidence here is messy and layered. Family tradition, immigration notes, and later commentary do not line up cleanly on every point. Some notes imply that three or four of Hannah's siblings died during the migration period, likely from disease, and that Hannah was among the very few surviving children by the time the family reached Utah. The biography itself says she was about nine when she crossed the plains, which would fit an **1855** arrival.

What matters most is not the exact company label so much as the family experience the biography preserves: storm-tossed sea travel, buried provisions, cold, sickness, hunger, and repeated death.

One remembered quotation, preserved through granddaughter **Evelyn Kofford Christensen**, is unforgettable. When food ran out,

> they scraped all the hair off the dog's hide and boiled it to make a broth, and Grandmother said they were so hungry that it tasted very delicious to them.

That is family memory, not an official trail log. But it is precisely the sort of memory families do not tend to invent casually. Whether every detail is literally exact or slightly telescoped by retelling, it captures the kind of extremity the family believed it had survived.

### In Mount Pleasant

By **1860** Hannah appears with her parents in **Mount Pleasant**, where the family entered the hard normalcy of early Sanpete settlement life. She was baptized into the LDS faith in **1857**, according to the compiled biography, and later remembered as having little formal schooling but a deep attachment to scripture.

Her granddaughter left a powerful image of her as an older woman:

> sitting with her Bible in her lap and tears in her eyes seeking the comfort she needed in her hours of sorrow.

That image helps explain the emotional cast of Hannah's story. Even in family memory she is not just cheerful pioneer stock. She is devout, burdened, and strong.

### Marriage and Household

Hannah married Jens on **10 November 1863**, according to the compiled history, and became the second wife in a household already marked by the remains of his earlier marriage. Her mother **Rasmine** died in **1866**, and her father later remarried. Those layered family structures — stepchildren, remarriages, migration grief, blended household memory — seem to have been normal parts of her adult world.

With Jens she had seven children:

- **Anthomina (Annie)**, born 23 Dec 1865  
- **Erastus (Jack)**, born 20 Mar 1868  
- **Sarah (Sadie)**, born 3 May 1870  
- **Hannah Margaret (Peggy)**, born 12 Aug 1872  
- **Olivia Mary**, born 24 Jan 1881  
- **Earl Henry**, born 11 Aug 1886  
- **Veda**, born 31 Aug 1888

The obituary and 1902 newspaper both independently support a child count of **seven**, which is unusually satisfying in a family history: a later compiled biography and contemporary newspapers converge.

### Farm, Music, and Sabbath Life

The Hannah biography gives the family home a warmer texture than Jens's more public story. On the farm they raised hay, grain, vegetables, potatoes, and cattle. The children attended the Birch Creek school when possible, and later some went into town for more education.

Granddaughter Evelyn's recollections describe a household with music and patterned religious life:
- Peggy on piano and guitar
- Olivia on mandolin
- Earl with his mouth organ
- Jack singing
- the family riding to church on Sundays

That is the kind of detail flat records never preserve and exactly why descendant prose matters.

### Hannah as Healer

The most striking section of Hannah's biography is not about migration or church quarrels but about nursing.

Family memory presents Hannah as a remarkably competent home healer. She nursed children through serious illness — **diphtheria**, **typhoid**, pneumonia, and what the story describes as a ruptured appendix or major abdominal abscess in infant Olivia.

One repeated anecdote says that when Olivia's throat was swollen and painful, Hannah would swab it again and again, telling the child, **"It will be better next time."** Another story says doctors had given Olivia up, but Hannah persisted with hot packs and homemade remedies until the crisis broke and the child survived.

Read literally, these stories can sound almost miraculous. Read historically, they sound like what many frontier mothers actually were: constant practical nurses in households where formal medical help was limited, delayed, expensive, or unsure.

Even if family memory sharpened the edge of these rescues over time, the cumulative picture is convincing. Hannah was remembered as the one who stayed awake, improvised, and refused to give up.

### Colorado, Return, and Church Estrangement

Hannah shared the Colorado colonization rupture with Jens, and her biography makes clear that the religious fallout was not just his private crisis. The family narrative says that after the bad experiences of the colony, Jens asked to have his name removed from church records and that Hannah followed him the next year.

This matters because it complicates the neat pioneer-Mormon story people often want. Here were faithful Scandinavian converts who crossed oceans and plains, helped settle Sanpete, raised their family in the Mormon world, and then — after colonization disappointments and accumulated grievances — stepped outside the church. Later family writers plainly believed this separation caused pain that echoed through descendants and that both eventually regretted it.

The 1930 funeral notice adds one more wrinkle: Hannah's funeral was held in the **Presbyterian church** in Mount Pleasant. That would have seemed strange if one were expecting an uncomplicated LDS end-of-life story, but it fits the estrangement preserved in family memory.

### Later Years: Town, Then Oregon

After Jens's death in **1902**, Hannah sold the town home in Mount Pleasant and eventually lived first with daughter **Sadie** in Vancouver, then more permanently with son **Earl** in **Medford, Oregon**. By the **1920** and **1930** censuses she is indeed in Medford.

Yet she did not disappear from Utah family memory. The compiled biography says she made visits back to Utah to see children and stepchildren. Her youngest daughter **Veda** — the direct-line ancestor here — married **Willard Hogan Kofford** in **1909**, while Hannah was living away.

She died in **Medford on 24 April 1930**, age 85, and was returned to **Mount Pleasant** for burial on **28 April 1930**.

Her obituary says she was survived by son **Earl** of Medford, son **Erastus** of Spanish Fork, daughter **Mrs. H. F. Wall** of Mount Pleasant, daughter **Mrs. Veda Kofford** of Provo, and notes that she and James had been the parents of seven children. The list is probably abbreviated, but its importance is that it ties the family memory back to public contemporary record.

Her funeral report is brief but respectful. The newspaper says the services were held at the Presbyterian church, and that the minister spoke on the subject **"A good woman, a good mother."** It is a simple line, but in Hannah's case it fits the evidence unusually well.

## Their Story Together

Jens and Hannah's marriage joined two strands of Danish Mormon immigration into one Sanpete family. Both came out of north Denmark. Both crossed into the harsh, improvised world of early Utah settlement. Both lived through the insecure years when fort walls, irrigation, livestock, and children all had to be made and defended at once.

Their life together had at least four distinct phases:

1. **early Mount Pleasant settlement**, with fort building and defense  
2. **the family-building years**, with children, farming, and local labor  
3. **Birch Creek prosperity**, with the larger farm, new frame house, and the younger children growing up  
4. **the later break**, when Colorado colonization and church estrangement altered the family's trajectory

If Jens is remembered for building — fort lines, brick, timber, fields, public usefulness — Hannah is remembered for enduring and healing. He appears in newspaper prose as a man of integrity. She appears in family prose as the woman who made broth from hide when starving and kept sick children alive when doctors had quit.

That pairing feels real: not saintly abstractions, but two kinds of frontier competence.

Their descendants remembered both the dignity and the wound in the story. They were faithful emigrants and settlers, yet the family also carried the memory that they ended estranged from the church they had sacrificed so much to join. Later compilers did not hide that fact. If anything, they wrote around it with visible sorrow.

That honesty makes the story stronger, not weaker.

## Notes on unresolved points

A few things remain unresolved and should stay visible:

- **Migration chronology for Jens**: the compiled biography emphasizes an 1857 Scandinavian company journey, while newspaper and GEDCOM note material point partly toward 1855-56 movement. This may reflect multiple travel stages, record confusion, or later compression, but it should not be silently harmonized.
- **Jens's first marriage**: the existence of the earlier marriage and children is clear in family compilation, but its exact chronology and legal details deserve a more primary-source check.
- **Marriage place for Jens and Hannah**: the year and likely date are strong; the exact place still needs firmer documentation in the extracted file set.
