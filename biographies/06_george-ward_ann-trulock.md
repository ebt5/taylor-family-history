=== Life Sketch ===
George spent his boyhood years living with his parents on a farm. During that phase of his life his activities were light in nature due to his poor health. George left the parental home to marry Ann Trulock. Employment as a proprietor of the Horse and Groom Inn at Woodbridge for six years and then as manager of a large mill which made linseed oil and oil cake resulted in prosperous beginnings for George and Ann. All seemed well with the Ward family. The family was growing in number, a close extended family relationship existed, and they were financially secure.
In 1847 the Ward family moved to Southhampton, Hampshire, England. In Southhampton George and Ann listened intently to Latter-day Saint missionaries testify of the restoration of the Gospel. After thoroughly investigating the message, they were baptized. (George was the first and only known member of his father's family to be converted to Mormonism.) This first step elicited such remarks as, "He did not have to leave to go to polygamy; he was doing so well." It is reported that George lost part of the family estate because he joined the Church. This loss was of little importance to him compared to the truth which he had found.
In 1851 the Ward family sailed from England to America. Their twelve-week voyage on the ship Ellen Maria ended at NEw Orleans on 6 April 1851. From New Orleans the Wards continued their journey up the Mississippi River and the Missouri River to Council Bluffs, Iowa. At Council Bluffs this family, consisting of George, age thirty-six, Ann, age thirty-four, and four children, ranging in age from one and a half to eight, outfitted themselves for crossing the plains to Zion. One ordeal of the journey, typical of those commonly experienced by pioneer families, was the birth of their son Moroni.
In June of 1851, while in Harris Grove, Ann gave birth to her fifth son, Moroni. It was raining hard, and we were forced to enter an old shed for shelter at ten p.m. Pans were held to catch the rain so as not to wet my wife.
The Ward family, suffering many hardships in their efforts to reach Zion, arrived in the Great Salt Lake valley on 9 October 1852.
In 1853 George and his family left the Salt Lake region in response to a call from the Prophet Brigham Young. They settled the new territory of North Willard Creek, now known as Willard City. This was Brigham Young's first call to George Welton Ward. His second call came on 30 June 1857. At this time Brigham Young married George to Mary Hankinson in his presidential office. This call gave George two wives, to which fifteen children were born.
In an effort to provide for his growing family in Willard, George became a farmer. These skills, learned as a youth, provided his family with the necessities of life. George prided himself in doing his farm work carefully and systematically. He trained his family of boys to be thorough in their work, and because of his training, the work of George Welton Ward's family drew attention among the early settlers. Edwin Cordon remarked, "There wasn't a man that could stack grain to shed water like George Welton Ward."
In addition to farming, George assisted in the community government of the pioneer settlement of Willard. He served as a commissioner of Box Elder County for twenty-five years, serving from 1857 to 1882. He was the estray pound keeper from 1864 to 1865. In addition, he was the mayor of Willard City for twelve years, from 1870 to 1882.
George's greatest contribution to those around him was not to be found in farming nor in community service. It was to be found in his priesthood callings. George served as first counselor to Bishop Cordon who was the first bishop of Willard Ward from 1857 to 1871. He was acting Bishop of Willard Ward from 1871 to 1877. On 9 September 1877 he was ordained Bishop of Willard Ward, holding this office until his death, 18 August 1882.
Line upon line, precept upon precept, the life of George Welton Ward grew. From the first step of baptism, to migration, to eternal marriage, to his calling as bishop, George was ready to accept eternal truths and was prepared to serve. His unswerving devotion, from his first contact with the missionaries to the end of his days in mortality, is an example to his posterity of one who held to the iron rod and glorified his Father in Heaven.

## Their Story Together

George Welton Ward and Ann Trulock were Latter-day Saint pioneers whose family became deeply woven into the fabric of Utah pioneer society. Ann Trulock immigrated to Deseret (Utah Territory) on 15 September 1852, and George had been a church member since at least 1814 records indicate his early affiliation.

George was one of those stalwart pioneer men who had multiple wives according to the practice of plural marriage in early Latter-day Saint culture; the family group records indicate Ann Trulock as his first wife, with Mary Hankinson as another. Their son Ernest Trulock Ward (named in honor of Ann's Trulock family surname) carried this heritage forward. The Ward family name persisted strongly in subsequent generations—Ernest Trulock Ward married Anna Elizabeth Barker, and their children carried the Ward legacy into the twentieth century.

Ann's immigration in 1852 placed her among the wave of British and Welsh converts who made the arduous journey to the Valley of the Great Salt Lake in the early territorial period.
