# Samuel Hutchinson & Frances Widdowson Goddard Buck
## Biography #13

**Relationship to Erik Taylor:** Ancestors connected through the Widdowson–Harris line. Frances Widdowson was the daughter of Sarah Ann Widdowson (later Sarah Ann Harris), placing this family as collateral ancestors intersecting the Matthew Harris line (Biography #8). Their descendants intermarried into Erik's extended family network.

## Samuel Hutchinson
**Born:** 7 June 1859, Hucknall Torkard, Nottinghamshire, England  
**Died:** 20 March 1895, Almy, Uinta, Wyoming, United States  
**Buried:** Almy Cemetery, Almy, Uinta, Wyoming  
**Parents:** Simeon Hutchinson and Sarah Ann Allen Hutchinson

## Frances Widdowson Goddard Buck
*(also known as Frances "Fanny" Harris Hutchinson; later Frances Bell)*  
**Born:** 15 August 1861, Hucknall Torkard, Nottinghamshire, England  
**Died:** 16 August 1944, Evanston, Uinta, Wyoming, United States  
**Buried:** Evanston City Cemetery, Evanston, Wyoming  
**Parents:** Sarah Ann Widdowson Goddard (biological); William Buck (biological father); later raised by stepfather Matthew Harris

**Married:** 14 November 1879, Basford, Nottinghamshire, England

---

## Origins in the Lace Country

Samuel Hutchinson and Frances Widdowson both grew up in Hucknall Torkard — a small industrial town in Nottinghamshire, England, embedded in the lace-making and coal-mining world of the East Midlands. Samuel was the youngest of ten children born to Simeon Hutchinson, a lace framework knitter. The 1861 census shows the family on Hucknall Lane. Samuel's baptism record of 4 August 1879 was later noted as lost, and he was rebaptized at Almy, Wyoming.

Frances had a more complicated beginning. She was born out of wedlock to Sarah Ann Widdowson Goddard, her biological father being William Buck. She was raised as Frances Harris after her mother married Matthew Harris (the same Matthew Harris of Biography #8 in this series). Family records confirm she was adopted by Matthew Harris and was enumerated in his household. The 1871 England census shows young Frances — listed as "Frances Harris, daughter, age 8, born Hucknall, Nottinghamshire" — living with Matthew Harris (frameknitter, age 36), Sarah Harris, and her siblings.

Samuel and Frances married in November 1879 in Basford. They were members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Two sons were born to them in Hucknall before the family made its pivotal decision to emigrate.

---

## Crossing the Atlantic: SS Nevada, 1884

On 9 April 1884, the Hutchinson family sailed from Liverpool aboard the steamship *SS Nevada*, arriving in New York on 19 April 1884. Samuel was 26; Frances was 24. With them were their two small sons, Samuel Alvin (age 3) and Abraham (age 1). They traveled to Ogden, Utah on 27 April 1884, accompanying other Latter-day Saint immigrants in the Mormon Immigration Index. Samuel's brother Abraham Hutchinson and his family made the same voyage.

The family did not remain in Utah. Following opportunities in the coal fields, they made their way to **Almy, Wyoming** — a Union Pacific coal-mining settlement in Uinta County. The discovery of vast coal reserves in southern Wyoming had driven the transcontinental railroad's route, and Almy had become a boomtown of miners from England, Scotland, and Scandinavia. Samuel went to work in the mines.

Other Hutchinson brothers — James and Thomas — followed from England and also settled at Almy.

---

## Life in Almy and Eight Children

The Hutchinson family grew steadily in Wyoming. Frances bore eight children in Wyoming from 1885 to 1893:

1. Samuel Alvin (1880–1925)
2. Abraham (1882–1956)
3. Sarah Ann Martin (1885–1929)
4. Ada Barker (1887–1964)
5. Charlotte (1889–1893) — died in childhood, buried with her father
6. William (1891–1971)
7. Esther (twin) (1893–1959)
8. Alice Lester (twin) (1893–1937)

Samuel obtained a homestead patent on Wyoming land, and the family built their life around the rhythms of the mining community.

---

## March 20, 1895: The Red Canyon Mine Disaster

On 20 March 1895, disaster struck the entire Hutchinson family and the Almy community. An explosion in Mine #5 at Red Canyon — just outside Almy — killed approximately 61 miners. Samuel Hutchinson was among the dead.

Devastatingly, Samuel was not alone. His brothers **James Hutchinson** and **Thomas Hutchinson** also perished in the same explosion, along with Thomas's son-in-law Samuel Bate. In a single afternoon, four men from one family were killed. The explosion left four Hutchinson widows and twenty-two Hutchinson children without fathers. When including extended family connections, the disaster directly affected five widows and twenty-eight children.

The *New York Times* covered the explosion. The community of Almy was shattered. The Hutchinson brothers — Samuel, James, and Thomas — were all buried in Almy Cemetery, along with many of the other victims.

Frances was left a widow at approximately 34 years of age, with eight young children, the youngest being twin girls just two years old.

---

## Frances Rebuilds: A Second Marriage and Six More Children

Frances — Fanny to those who knew her — was not one to be broken by grief. After several years of widowhood, she married **Joseph Edward Bell** on 22 April 1896 in Evanston, Wyoming. Joseph Bell (1864–1913) became stepfather to her eight children. Together they had six more children:

1. Annie Marsh Mayling (1896–1974)
2. George Walter (1899–1970)
3. Clarence (1901–1986)
4. Frances Lester (1903–1979)
5. Joseph (1905–1966)
6. Earl (1907–1976)

In a remarkable convergence, three of Frances's daughters eventually married three brothers of the Lester family. Samuel's twins Esther and Alice married James Henry Lester and William Powell Lester respectively, and Frances's daughter Frances Bell married Merle Lester. Census records show the Hutchinson/Bell family and the Lester family living side by side across nearly every census available.

Joseph Bell died in 1913. Frances outlived two husbands and survived until 16 August 1944, dying the day after her 83rd birthday in Evanston, Wyoming. She is buried in Evanston City Cemetery on a shared headstone with Joseph Bell.

---

## The Almy Legacy

The *Almy Centennial History Book* (1990) dedicated pages 123–125 to Samuel and Frances Hutchinson — a testament to their place in Wyoming pioneer memory. Samuel's baptism, their sea voyage, his coal-mining life, and his death in the disaster all became part of the woven history of this community.

The Red Canyon explosion of 1895 stands as one of Wyoming's early industrial tragedies, a reminder of the dangers that shaped the lives of thousands of Latter-day Saint immigrants who came west seeking a new Zion, only to find themselves in the coalfields and mining camps that powered the growing nation.
