## John Joseph Ward: Utah Native, Railroad Builder, Farmer

John Joseph Ward was born 23 January 1854 in Willard, Box Elder County, Utah Territory — a native son of the Utah pioneer community. His father, George Welton Ward, was among the founding settlers of Willard (see Biography #6). John grew up working the land beside his father.

*"Spiritual strength and devotion to God were the foundations upon which John Joseph Ward built his life,"* his family history records. *"These characteristics were learned from working side by side with his father in the fields of Willard. While planting, John J. learned the value of living a life worthy of being called a Latter-day Saint. From the harvest he reaped not only a livelihood but also a testimony of the divinity of Jesus Christ."*

---

## Building the Transcontinental Railroad

Before he settled into farming, young John J. Ward participated in one of the defining events of 19th-century American history: the completion of the transcontinental railroad.

As a boy, he **assisted in building the first continental railroad**, working in the fall of 1868 and the spring of 1869 — the very months when the Central Pacific and the Union Pacific were connected at **Promontory Point in Box Elder County, Utah**. Promontory was just a few dozen miles from Willard. John was 14–15 years old when the Golden Spike was driven on 10 May 1869.

He then served **three years on the Utah Northern Railroad** in construction work, connecting the rails with the Union Pacific in Ogden. He was ordained an Elder and a Seventy on **8 December 1874**.

---

## Ellenor Owens: A Name Made Unique

Ellenor Mary Owens was born 14 March 1859 in American Fork — a few weeks before her family moved to Willard (her father Owen Owens relocated the family to Willard in April 1859). So while she was born in American Fork, Willard was her home and her world.

Her real name was **Mary Ellen Owens** — but Willard had a naming problem. As her son J. Welton Ward explained in the minutes of the George Welton Ward Family Organization (6 June 1953):

> *"There were three living in Willard at the time whose name was Mary Ellen: 1-Mary Ellen Brewerton; 2-Mary Ellen (Owen) Ward; 3-Mary Ellen Lowe. Thus to help separate these women she opted to go by Eleanor M."*

In census records she appears as **Mary E.** In family histories she is **Ellenor** or **Eleanor M.** On her headstone she is **J.J. Ward and his wife Ellenor Owens Ward.** The name "Ellenor" became her identity even though she was christened something else entirely.

She was rebaptized on **13 October 1877** by George Facer — a common practice in the early Utah church.

---

## Marriage and the House in Willard

John Joseph Ward and Mary Eleanor Owens were married on **27 October 1876** in the Endowment House in Salt Lake City — a temple-adjacent facility used for sacred ordinances before the Salt Lake Temple was completed.

John immediately set to work building a home. *"With his own hands he built a two-story dwelling in Willard. This sturdy edifice became the site of joyful music, home industry, and abundant love."* As John tilled the fields, Eleanor cared for the home. The house became what was described as *"one of the most well-kept homes in the community."*

For **sixty-two years** — from 1876 to Eleanor's death in 1940 — this couple lived in that Willard home. The house was photographed in 1973 (John had then been dead for 32 years), and a separate photograph shows the Kofford barn beside the home. The family's 60th anniversary gathering was photographed there.

---

## Community Service: Mayor, County Treasurer, Railroad Commissioner

John J. Ward's public service was extraordinary for a farmer in a small Utah town:

- **Councilman of Willard**, 1886 and 1887
- **Mayor of Willard**, 1888–1889
- **City councilman** again, serving six additional years
- **Box Elder County Treasurer**, elected fall 1918
- **Trustee, Willard Central School District**, eight years
- **Organizer, director, and president, Willard Irrigation Company**
- **Organizer and officer, Fruit Growers' Association**
- **Member, Box Elder Commercial Club**, Brigham City
- Took active part in committee to sell **war savings stamps and victory bonds** for Box Elder County during World War I

He was a man who shaped his community at every level — local government, water rights, agriculture, education, and wartime service.

---

## A Mission to England

After six of his children were born — and three had died — John J. received a mission call to England. He left on **17 December 1890**, served as a traveling Elder in Great Britain, and returned on **20 March 1893** — nearly two and a half years away.

During his absence, Eleanor managed the home and children. The community of Willard supported them. When John returned, he resumed both his farming and his public service.

---

## Church Life: Twenty-Five Years in Sunday School

*"For over twenty-five years he served the Willard Ward faithfully as superintendent of the Sunday School."*

In 1888 he accepted a position as treasurer of the building committee for the Willard Ward. His church service was embedded in everyday life. His son Karl recalled the nightly ritual: *"Each night the chairs in their farmhouse were turned around at the dining room table in readiness for family prayer in the morning."*

When Karl collected fast offerings as a Deacon, his father John J. would *"contribute bottled fruit, a sack of wheat, potatoes, and coal or flour."*

Karl's tribute to his parents is preserved:

> *"I am so grateful for my pioneer heritage, and the great pioneer spirit of helping thy neighbor that permeated the little town of Willard, Utah. I am so grateful for an ideal mother and a faithful, understanding father. My parents gave me the great teaching of love thy neighbor, fair play, and the precious gospel principles by example. Their splendid example will always be revered."*

---

## Deaths: One Day Apart, Sixty-Two Years Married

Eleanor Owens Ward died on **31 December 1940** in Salt Lake City — New Year's Eve. Her obituary appeared in Brigham City papers. She was buried 2 January 1941 in Willard City Cemetery.

John Joseph Ward died just **21 days later**, on **21 January 1941**, in Willard. He had been married to Eleanor for sixty-two years. He was 86 years old. His death certificate is preserved (Utah State Death Certificate, Willard).

Their shared headstone reads: *"John J. Ward and his wife Ellenor Owens Ward."*

They lie in the Willard City Cemetery — the community they had served, farmed, governed, and loved for their entire adult lives.

---

## Legacy

John Joseph Ward and Ellenor Mary Owens were Utah's second generation — born in the Territory, not in England or Wales or Denmark. They were shaped entirely by pioneer culture, by the rhythms of farming and faith, by the history their parents had carried across the plains.

John helped build the transcontinental railroad as a teenager. He ran the town government as a young man. He served a mission to the country his father had emigrated from. He built with his own hands, farmed with his own hands, and shaped a community that still exists today in northern Utah.

Their story is one of steadiness and depth — not the dramatic arc of emigration or captivity or gold rushes, but the equally important story of what it meant to plant roots in the Utah that the first generation had sacrificed to reach.
