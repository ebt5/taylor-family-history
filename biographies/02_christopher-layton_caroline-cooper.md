## Christopher Layton

**Born:** March 8, 1821, Thorncote (Thorncut), Northill Parish, Bedfordshire, England
**Died:** August 7, 1898, Kaysville, Davis County, Utah

Christopher Layton was born the youngest son of Samuel Layton and Isabella Wheeler in the village of Thorncote, a small hamlet in Bedfordshire, England. His father, Samuel, was a labourer. Due to family financial pressures, Christopher was sent to work at age eight, earning thirty-three cents a week to help support the family.

His life changed irrevocably on January 1, 1842, when he was baptized a member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in Thorncote by Elder Samuel Howard. He was twenty years old. Six months later, on July 10, 1842, he married Mary Matthews. In January 1843 the couple — newly converted, newly married — sailed for America on the vessel *Swanton* with a company of Latter-day Saints under the direction of Lorenzo Snow.

### Nauvoo

Christopher arrived in Nauvoo, Illinois in 1843 and met the Prophet Joseph Smith on the banks of the Mississippi River. He underwent the persecutions that beset the Nauvoo Saints from 1843 to 1846. He served as a temple guard. On January 31, 1846, he received his endowment and was sealed to his wife Mary in the Nauvoo Temple — one of the last ordinances performed there before the Saints were driven west. His first wife Mary died, and their infant son also died during the Nauvoo period. Christopher left his surviving daughter with friends and enlisted.

### The Mormon Battalion

In July 1846 at Council Bluffs, Iowa Territory, Christopher Layton enlisted as Private No. 47 in Company C of the Mormon Battalion — a volunteer military unit of approximately 500 Latter-day Saint men recruited by the United States Army during the Mexican-American War. He was 25 years old.

The Battalion marched roughly 2,000 miles from Council Bluffs through Santa Fe and Tucson to San Diego — the longest infantry march in American military history. Christopher's specific service included duty as a teamster. While the Battalion was stationed in San Diego, he carried the mail between San Diego and Los Angeles and back, a round-trip of 250 miles. When provisions ran short, an eight-mule team was dispatched to San Diego — Christopher drove it. He was discharged July 16, 1847, at Ciudad de los Angeles (present-day Los Angeles). The dismissal was unceremonious: Lieutenant Smith simply marched down the line and said, "You are discharged." No ceremony, no acknowledgment of the journey. Christopher found the brevity of it fitting.

### California, England, and Return

In spring 1849, Christopher went to Sutter's ranch and tried his hand at gold digging. He stayed three days — decided he didn't like it — then bought a band of horses in Southern California for $1.50 each, drove them to San Francisco, and sold several for up to $100 apiece. With his earnings he bought a ship's passage back to England to visit his family. He was away only briefly. He married Sarah Martin (sister of his brother-in-law Samuel Martin) in England on May 3, 1850, and returned to America.

Back in the United States, Christopher spent two years in St. Louis before crossing the plains to the Salt Lake Valley. By the 1850 Utah Federal Census he reported real wealth of $1,000 and personal wealth of $3,800. He settled in Kaysville, Davis County, Utah.

### Utah: Family, Church, and Community

Christopher Layton's Utah life was shaped by polygamy, by the frontier economy, and by continuous Church service. He married multiple times under the doctrine of plural marriage:

- Mary Matthews (d. Nauvoo)
- Sarah Martin (married England, 1850)
- Sarah Barnes (September 25, 1852)
- Isabella Golightly
- **Caroline Cooper** (April 12, 1856) — Erik's line
- Several additional wives

He operated butcher shops in West Jordan, Salt Lake County, in the early 1850s, then moved to Kaysville. He served as Bishop of Kaysville Ward from 1861 to 1874 — thirteen years of presiding over a frontier community. He was later called as a Stake President in the St. Joseph Stake in Arizona.

He was also a militia officer: Brigade Quartermaster, Nauvoo Legion, 1866.

**The town of Layton, Utah** — now a city of 80,000+ — was named in his honor.

Christopher Layton died August 7, 1898 in Kaysville. He was 77 years old. His autobiography, published posthumously, remained a source document for his family into the 20th century.

## Caroline Cooper

**Born:** September 26, 1836, Rotherham (Scholes area), South Yorkshire, England
**Died:** October 1, 1912

Caroline Cooper was born in the Scholes district of Rotherham, South Yorkshire — a working-class industrial area in northern England known for coal mining and iron working. Her family were framework knitters and colliers.

She was baptized a Latter-day Saint on August 18, 1846 by Elder Carter, attending the Derbyshire Conference of the Church. She was ten years old. The region was actively missionized by Latter-day Saint elders throughout the 1840s, and her family evidently converted together.

She emigrated to Utah as part of the great Mormon migration, crossing the Atlantic and the plains to reach the Salt Lake Valley. The Cooper family was among thousands of English converts who made this journey during the 1850s. Her mother, Christina (née Darrin), came to America in 1850 on the ship *Argo*; her father James followed separately.

Caroline married Christopher Layton on April 12, 1856 in Kaysville, Utah — she was 19, he was 35 and already had multiple wives. This was a plural marriage under the practice then observed by many Latter-day Saints. She bore Christopher at least five children, including:

- **Cynthia Abigail Layton** (b. 1867) — Erik's direct ancestor

Caroline outlived her husband by fourteen years, dying October 1, 1912 at approximately 76 years of age.

## Their Story Together

Christopher Layton and Caroline Cooper represent the collision of two very different journeys to the same destination. Christopher came from Bedfordshire, walked to Los Angeles with an army, rode horses to San Francisco, sailed to England and back — a man shaped by extraordinary mobility and adventure. Caroline came from the Yorkshire coalfields as a ten-year-old convert, crossed the ocean in steerage, crossed the plains by wagon.

They met in Kaysville, Utah, a frontier settlement that Christopher helped establish and lead. Their marriage in 1856 was Caroline's first and Christopher's fourth or fifth. She gave him children and a household in a community built from almost nothing in desert country. Their daughter Cynthia Abigail carried the Layton line forward to the next generation.

The town of Layton, Utah bears his name today. His granddaughter **Phoebe Caroline Layton** (b. 1896, d. not recorded) was named after her grandmother Caroline — suggesting the depth of Caroline's presence in the family memory. Phoebe Caroline Layton was Erik's great-grandmother.
