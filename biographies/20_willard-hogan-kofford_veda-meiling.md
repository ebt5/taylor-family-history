## Two Sanpete County Families

Willard Hogan Kofford and Veda Meiling were both born in Mount Pleasant, Sanpete County, Utah — the same small agricultural community in central Utah where both their families had settled as part of the Scandinavian LDS immigration wave. Willard was the son of Danish-born Willard Kofford (sr.) and Swedish-born Christena Neilson (Biography #18). Veda was the daughter of Jens James Christian Meiling, himself a Danish immigrant.

Mount Pleasant in the 1880s was a close-knit community of farmers, fruit growers, and devout Latter-day Saints. It was the world Willard and Veda grew up in — a world of orchards, hard labor, and deep community faith.

---

## Willard's Early Years: Growing Up "Bill"

Known to everyone as "Bill," Willard Hogan Kofford grew up as the older of two brothers — Clarence Louis Kofford (born 1887) being the younger. Their early childhood was spent on the family's dairy farm out in the hay fields about three miles west of Chester, Sanpete County.

From Clarence's family history, a vivid portrait of young "Bill" emerges:

> *"Willard his older brother started school. Clarence wanted to attend with his brother. To discourage this, Bill, as Willard was nicknamed, told Clarence how the teachers were so mean and would beat up the children. When the time came for Clarence to start school at Wasatch Academy, he was so frightened of what the teacher might do, his father had to take him and stay there with him each day until he became adjusted."*

Another beloved family story — the molasses candy incident — captures the mischief of the Kofford boys:

> *"One night while the folks were away, Clarence and Bill decided to make molasses candy. Their mother had told them not to before she left. Some of their friends were there, so they decided to go ahead. As they were taking it off the stove, it was accidentally spilled on Clarence's bare foot. They poured cold water on the foot and when they tried to pull the candy off of Clarence's foot the flesh came too. No one was to tell what had happened. When the folks returned Mother heard Clarence moaning. She went in and asked what was wrong. Bill replied in his sleep: 'Oh, we spilled hot molasses candy on Clarence's foot.'"*

Clarence was out of school for six weeks.

## The Move to Provo Bench / Orem

When the Kofford family moved north from Sanpete in 1896 — eventually settling on the Provo Bench and in what would become Orem — young Willard Hogan came with them. The family established a fruit farm and, later, a store. Bill's name is attached to what became **the first service station in Orem** — "Bill's Service Station." Family photographs preserved in FamilySearch show the Kofford store and station on State Street, the family home beside it, and the reflection of the Wasatch Mountains in the store window.

This store and station became a landmark of early Orem — the peach orchards his father planted giving way to commerce as the Provo Bench urbanized in the early 20th century.

---

## Marriage to Veda Meiling, 1909

Willard and Veda were married on **20 July 1909** in Provo, Utah. The marriage license was noted in *The Salt Lake Herald* — a testament to how even routine community events were tracked in the Utah press of the era. Veda was 20; Willard was 24.

Veda Meiling brought Danish heritage to the family: her father Jens James Christian Meiling had been born 17 April 1834 in Aasted, Hjorring, Denmark, and emigrated to Utah as part of the LDS Scandinavian conversion movement. Photographs from 1944 show Veda Meiling Kofford with her sister Olivia Meiling, taken in Orem — two women who had grown up in the same community their parents helped build.

## Their Family

To Willard and Veda was born one daughter documented directly in the family tree:

- **Veda Alene Kofford** (1911–1999) — *Erik Taylor's grandmother*

Alene, as she was known, grew up in the Kofford household on the Provo Bench in Orem, surrounded by her parents' store and station, the orchards, and the deep-rooted Latter-day Saint community life. A family photograph shows Willard and Veda with five children (suggesting more children than just Alene), though Alene is the documented line.

---

## Alene's Love Story — In Her Own Words

Veda Alene Kofford wrote "The Story of My Life" — a memoir that captures the moment she met Paul Hector Taylor. The note preserved in the family GEDCOM begins:

> *(Taken from "The Story of My Life" by Alene Kofford Taylor)*  
> *"During the summer of 1931 I received a phone call one fine day from a person who identified himself as Paul Taylor. I doubted that this..."*

The courtship between Alene Kofford and Paul Hector Taylor — and their marriage — linked the Kofford line to the Taylor line. A family photograph labeled **"Alene and Paul's Wedding Day"** shows Alene and Paul with Alene's parents, **Bill and Veda Kofford** — the grandparents of Byron Taylor, the father of Erik Taylor.

---

## A Portrait of Bill and Veda's Later Years

Willard Hogan Kofford (Bill) registered for the World War II draft on 27 April 1942 in Provo — at age 57, he was beyond combat age, but draft registration extended to all men in those years. His WWII draft card lists **Veda Kofford** as his contact.

The couple continued to live in Orem through the 1940s and 1950s. Veda Meiling Kofford died **28 May 1962** in Provo at age 73. She was buried 31 May 1962 in the Orem Cemetery. Her obituary appeared in the *Orem-Geneva Times* dated 31 May 1962.

Willard Hogan Kofford — Bill — died just over a year later, on **19 July 1963** in Orem, at age 78. He was buried 22 July 1963 in Orem.

Their daughter Alene's memoir, written about her life and marriage to Paul Taylor, stands as the most personal document connecting this generation of Koffords to Erik's family.

---

## Legacy

Willard Hogan Kofford and Veda Meiling are Erik Taylor's great-grandparents — the family connection through Byron Taylor's mother Veda Alene Kofford. Bill's service station stood as one of the first in Orem. Their family home on State Street and the Kofford orchard/farm were part of the transformation of the Provo Bench from farmland to suburb.

The Kofford name in Orem traces back two full generations: from Willard (sr.) and Christena who cleared the orchards, to Bill and Veda who ran the store and raised Alene, to Alene who met Paul Taylor in 1931 and began the next chapter of the family's story.
