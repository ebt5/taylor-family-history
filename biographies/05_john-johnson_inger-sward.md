=== Life Sketch ===
John Johnson
1849 - 1936

DEATH TAKES UTAH PIONEER
Lake View Ward Bishop
Succumbs at 87

PROVO--John Johnson, 87, Utah pioneer and bishop of the L. D. S. Lake View [sic] ward 22 years, died Wednesday morning of causes incident to age.

Mr. Johnson was born June 7, 1849, at Lorten, Hedemarken, Norway, [sic] a son of John and Bergette [sic] Larsen Johnson. His parents joined the L. D. S. church in Norway and came to this country in 1864, crossing the plains by ox team.

In 1868 John Johnson was called to drive an ox team back to immigrants. The trip was made with 61 wagons, with a total of 650 immigrants being brought to Salt Lake City. Mr. Johnson met his future wife, Inger Sward, on this trip, and they were married June 13, 1870, in the old L. D. S. endowment house at Salt Lake City.

Mr. Johnson served a mission fo rthe L. D. S. church in his native land from 1889 to 1892. He was ordained a bishop of the L. D. S. Lake View ward on February 14, 1893, by President Joseph F. Smith. He retired from the bishopric September 26, 1915, after having served 22 years. Shortly after he moved to the Provo L. D. S. Third ward, where he was active in church capacities. He was ordained president of the Utah L. D. S. stake high priests' quorum on June 6, 1920.

Surviving are two sons and four daughters, Alfred H. Johnson, August Johnson, Mrs. L. L. Bunnell, Mrs. M. A. Clinger, Lake View; Mrs. H. E. Hatton, Salt Lake City; Mrs. W. W. Goodridge, Provo; one foster-daughter, Mrs. Jack McCune, Torrance, Cal.; 41 grandchildren, 82 great-grandchildren; one brother and one sister, Andrew Knudson, Provo, and Mrs. Peter Madsen, Lake View.

Funeral services will be conducted Sunday at 1:30 p. m. in the Lake View [sic] ward chapel. Friends may call at the Berg Mortuary Saturday and at the Alfred Johnson residence in Lake View prior to the services. Burial will be in the Provo City Burial Park.

- Salt Lake Tribune | Salt Lake City, Utah | 12 June 1936 | p. 10 | transcribed by Annie Duckett Hundley.

## Their Story Together

John Johnson and Inger Akkesdotter Sward represent the Scandinavian pioneer stream that flowed into Utah during the latter half of the nineteenth century. Inger, also known as Ingar Akesdotter or Ingrid Åkesdotter, was of Swedish heritage—her patronymic surname "Akkesdotter" indicating she was the daughter of Ake (or Akke). The Sward family connection suggests she came from that Swedish family group.

John worked as a dairy farmer, a common occupation in the agricultural communities of pioneer Utah. Together they built a family that would connect to the broader Taylor and Naylor ancestry. Their story reflects the hardships and triumphs of Scandinavian converts who left their homeland in response to the Latter-day Saint missionary work in Scandinavia and emigrated to build a new life in the American West.
