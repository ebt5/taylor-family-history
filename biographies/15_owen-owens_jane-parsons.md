## Owen Owens

**Born:** December 21, 1836, Merionethshire (Meirionnydd), North Wales
**Died:** June 13, 1913, Willard, Box Elder, Utah (age 76)
**Parents:** William Owens and Eleanor Evans Owens

### Wales: The Owens Family

Owen Owens was born December 21, 1836 in Merionethshire — the rugged slate-quarrying and sheep-farming region of northwest Wales. His father William was a substantial farmer, renting a large tract of land for ninety pounds per year, keeping twenty milk cows and as many as two thousand head of sheep.

In 1848, LDS missionaries brought the gospel to Merionethshire. The entire William Owens family accepted baptism at Festiniog, Wales. The baptisms were recorded by John D. Peters's father: William Owen on September 17, Magdalene and Richard Owens on October 12, Ellen and Cadwalader Owens on October 15, Owen Owens and Catherine Owens on November 5, William Owens Jr. and Alice Owens on November 20. All performed at Festiniog. An old woman named Roberts who was still living in Peters's father's home decades later pointed out the exact spot below the old waterwheel of the carding and spinning factory where those baptisms were done.

Two months after Owen's baptism, on December 15, 1848, his sisters Magdalene (age 17) and Catherine (likely Owen's twin, age 12) died on the same Friday and were buried in the same grave on Monday.

### The Mississippi Cholera Catastrophe (1849)

The family's decision to emigrate to Zion led to one of the most devastating family tragedies in this history. In spring 1849, nine members of the William Owens family departed for America. As they traveled up the Mississippi River, cholera struck.

As recorded in John D. Peters's father's Welsh-language memoranda book, and transcribed in a letter Peters sent to Jane after Owen's funeral:

- **Jane Owens** (daughter) — died Monday, May 7, 1849, buried on the banks of the Mississippi
- **William Owens Jr.** (son) — died Tuesday, May 8
- **Alice Owens** (daughter) — died Tuesday, May 8, the same day as her brother
- **Eleanor Owens** (the mother) — died Wednesday, May 9
- **William Owens Sr.** (the father) — died Friday, May 12
- **Richard Owens** (son) — died Friday, May 18 — the following week

Six people in eleven days. Father, mother, and four children, buried on the riverbank. Owen was thirteen years old. Only three survived: Owen, his twenty-year-old sister, and his twenty-four-year-old brother. Peters, writing to Jane in 1913, called Eleanor Owens "a woman of wonderful character and will power" — noting that she had set out with an invalid husband and seven children to a far western land where everything was undeveloped and she could not speak the language. "She must have been a mighty brave woman."

The three survivors traveled on to Council Bluffs, Iowa, outfitted themselves with one yoke of cows and two yokes of oxen, and arrived in the Salt Lake Valley in time for the fall conference of 1849. Owen was thirteen years old and had just watched most of his family die.

### Building a Life

That first winter, Owen and his surviving siblings lived in a dugout across the Jordan River in a Welsh colony. The older two soon married — and Owen was left entirely on his own.

His early Utah years were years of labor:
- **Spring 1850**: Hired by John Mercer for eight months at $8 per month for farm work south of Salt Lake City. He was fourteen.
- **Fall 1850**: Went with Captain Dan Jones to Sanpete County for a year of farm labor
- **Winter 1851**: Lived with his married sister in Salt Lake City; attended school
- **Summer 1852**: Farmed for John Bauch in Salt Lake City
- **February 1853**: Moved to American Fork — where he met Jane Parsons

He first saw Jane at a dance. His grandson William W. Owens, writing in 1958: *"One of his daughters asked him when he first met Jane. He said it was at a dance and he was dancing in his bare feet."* About all he had at the time of their marriage was a small Indian pony — which he traded to a soldier at Camp Floyd for a yoke of oxen.

## Jane Parsons

**Born:** October 13, 1842, Nauvoo, Hancock County, Illinois
**Died:** July 21, 1922, Willard, Box Elder, Utah (age 79)
**Also known as:** "Grandmother Owens" (family)
**Parents:** William Parsons (b. March 27, 1807, Worcestershire, England; d. 1882, Willard, Utah) and Mary Jenkins Parsons (b. September 28, 1813, Herefordshire, England; d. August 13, 1892, Willard, Utah)

*Note: Jane was married October 2, 1857 — eleven days before her 15th birthday. She was 14 years old.*

### The Parsons Family: English Converts in Nauvoo

William Parsons and Mary Jenkins had been Welsh Methodists, then joined a sect called the Ranters — a group of six hundred or more with their own minister and chapel. They were *"very earnest in their belief and yet they were not just satisfied as they felt there was something lacking."*

When **Elder Wilford Woodruff** came to their village in spring 1840 with the gospel, their hearts were prepared. They and the entire branch were baptized by Woodruff in March 1840. They then left England the following September on the sailing vessel *North America* — *"the first shipload of saints that left England for the gospel's sake,"* as Jane would later write. They landed at New Orleans and came by river boat to Nauvoo, arriving sometime in November 1840.

Jane was born there in October 1842. She grew up in Nauvoo during the most turbulent years of early Mormonism.

### Nauvoo: Earliest Memories

Jane's earliest memories: men carrying guns, the Nauvoo Legion drilling constantly, state officers harassing the Prophet Joseph Smith.

And the Temple. White limestone. Facing west. Small columns running up each side of the doors and windows, and on top of each column a block of stone with a man's face carved in it. She remembers her father lifting her up on his shoulders in a large crowd, and she said to him: *"Look at those men's faces on the front of the temple."*

After Joseph Smith was killed, the Parsons family was driven out by the mob. They settled at Farmington on the Des Moines River for one winter, then moved to Winter Quarters — then went to work for a Frenchman named Bushey near Kanesville (now Council Bluffs), where Bushey had large cattle herds and her father worked as a herdsman. They stayed five years, saved, and outfitted: one wagon, two yokes of oxen, one yoke of cows.

They started for Utah sometime in June 1852. **They arrived August 23, 1852.** Jane was nine years old. The family stayed a week in Salt Lake City, then went south to American Fork, where they lived for seven years.

In the spring of 1857 — Jane actually writes "1857" but appears to mean 1859 based on other records — they sold their American Fork home and moved to **Willard, Utah**, then called "North Willow Creek" (renamed Willard in the early 1860s). Her parents lived there until they died.

### Childhood Skills

Jane wrote with understated pride about what she learned before she was married:

*"From a child up to my marriage I learned to sew, knit, crochet, braid straw (wheat) to make into hats for men to wear in summer, spin wool into yarn to weave to make into jeans for men's coats and pants, weave linsey for women's and children's dresses and blankets and underwear. I also doubled and twisted and colored the yarn as all the yarn had to be colored before being knit into stockings for all the family. I being older than my brother had to help father with field work such as planting corn and potatoes, raking and bunching hay, hauling and stacking. After the wheat was hauled we would go gleaning. After the harvest we would go on the bench to gather wood as the canyons were not open very much at this early date."*

*"The day before I went to get married I helped haul the last load of hay for that season."*

She had very little formal schooling. She taught herself letters from Webster's Elementary Spelling Book. She went to school at age eight or nine for two short terms taught by two ladies. After arriving in Utah, she attended off and on until she was fifteen — then married. She and Owen went to school together that first winter for a few months.

## Marriage and Family in Willard

Owen Owens and Jane Parsons were **married and sealed on October 2, 1857** in the Endowment House at Salt Lake City — by President **Brigham Young**. Jane was fourteen years and eleven days old.

They lived with Jane's parents for about three years. When the Parsons family moved to Willard, Owen and Jane went with them.

Owen had traded his Indian pony for a yoke of oxen. He then traded those oxen to his father-in-law William Parsons for **eight acres of land**. Owen's grandson William W. Owens noted: *"My father bought part of that land when he was a young man and we later sold it for $300 per acre."*

In 1861, Owen got logs out of Willard Canyon and built their first log house — the logs still standing decades later. Nails cost a dollar a pound; the floor was laid with wooden pegs. The wooden roof was the first of its kind in Willard in 1860.

In the spring of 1867, Owen and his father-in-law bought another farm and divided the land — the west farm and pasture.

**Children of Owen Owens and Jane Parsons** (nine born):
1. **Ellenor Mary Owens** — born March 26, 1858 (or March 14, 1859 per another record), American Fork, Utah — married John Joseph Ward *(Erik's ancestor)*
2. Esther Jane Owens — born May 27, 1862, Willard
3. William Parsons Owens — born November 16, 1864, Willard
4. John Frederic Owens — born April 14, 1867, Willard
5. Owen Oscar Owens — born July 17, 1869, Willard
6. Minnie Elizabeth Owens — born December 8, 1871, Willard
7. Margaret Olive Owens — born March 25, 1879, Willard
8. Sarah Charlotte Owens — born October 5, 1877, Willard
9. Kate Ann Owens — born June 17, 1880, Willard

In **February 1871**, Owen took a second wife: **Martha Ann Waite** (born March 5, 1843, Garferton, Lincolnshire, England). They had eight children together. Martha Ann died August 9, 1888.

Owen ran a milk wagon from Willard to Pleasant View for seven years. He farmed throughout his life. He served as **assistant Sunday School superintendent** and as a **bishop's counselor** in the Willard Ward.

### The Horse-Trader Story

Jane's grandson William W. Owens, writing in 1958, included a story worth preserving:

*"Trading horses, land and other things was common at that time. Some men were good traders. Everyone knew that one had to beware in trading horses. My Uncle John Owens, Father's brother, became a farmer and he knew horses. It is said that he once traded a horse to another man but he told the man plainly the horse was not a 'good looker.' In a few days the man came back and told him the horse was blind. My Uncle said, 'I told you he was not a good looker.'"*

## Jane's Church Life

Jane's record of service, written in her own words in 1915, is extraordinary:

- **First counselor to the Relief Society president for twenty-five years or more**
- **President of the first Primary Association organized in Willard for twenty years**
- Sunday School teacher for thirty-five to forty years
- Member of the Genealogical Society of Salt Lake City

On October 19, 1870, she went with her parents to the Endowment House in Salt Lake City — then located at the northwest corner of the Temple Block — and did a great deal of work for the dead. She continued this work more or less to the end of her life.

She was rebaptized November 9, 1876, by John T. Ward, and reconfirmed by her father William Parsons Sr. that same month. She received her Patriarchal Blessing from Patriarch John Smith on December 15, 1881, in Willard — lineage of Ephraim.

## Owen's Death

Owen Owens was confined to his bed with pneumonia for four days. He died at **11:15 p.m., Friday, June 13, 1913** at age 76.

Just six months earlier — December 30, 1912 — he had given his life's account to his grandson William W. Owens, the very history that has preserved so much of what we know.

His funeral was held in the Willard Tabernacle. The music chosen: "Come, Come, Ye Saints," "I Know that My Redeemer Lives," and "By Their Fruits Shall Ye Know Them" — what his family history calls *"an appropriate eulogy to this illustrious forebear."*

He had outlived his second wife Martha by twenty-five years and would outlive his first son William Parsons Owens, who died of typhoid fever at twenty-nine.

The headstone in Willard City Cemetery reads:
> **OWEN** / DEC 21, 1836 / JUNE 13, 1913
> **JANE P.** / OCT. 10, 1842 / JULY 6, 1905 *(dates on headstone vary from records)*
> **MARTHA** / MAR. 2, 1843 / AUG. 9, 1888
> **OWENS**

## Jane's Final Years and Death

After Owen's death, Jane continued to live in Willard. In January 1915, at age 72, she wrote her own history for her grandson William W. Owens — seven years before her death. The document is meticulous, warm, and unsentimental.

She noted her children and grandchildren:
- Nine children born; five living at the time of writing
- Forty-two grandchildren; thirty-three living
- Eleven great-grandchildren; nine living

At her funeral there was a **65-year-old daughter** present, along with many others of her nine children and their families.

**Jane Parsons Owens died July 21, 1922** in Willard, Box Elder, Utah. Cause of death: myocarditis due to chronic nephritis. She was 79 years old. Buried in the Willard City Cemetery, July 23, 1922.

She had lived long enough to see her grandchildren settled, her missionary grandson's diary treasured, and the church she and her parents had walked away from England to join become firmly rooted in the Utah soil she had helped plow.

Her grandson William W. Owens, writing about her in 1958: *"She was married to Owen Owens when she was 14 — eleven days before her 15th birthday. She lived until she was 80. She had a 65 year old daughter at her funeral plus many others of her 9 children and their children."*

## Owen's Family: What the Peters Letter Adds

The letter John D. Peters wrote to Jane on June 18, 1913 — five days after Owen's funeral — gives a rare close-up of what Owen's family went through. Peters had found, in his father's Welsh-language memoranda book, the record of every baptism and every death of the Owens family from Festiniog. He copied them out and sent them to Jane with this observation:

*"I have heard my mother say that William Owens and his wife were a splendid type of humanity. That Ellenor, as mother called her, was a woman of wonderful character and will power. When we stop to reflect, it does not need any testimony to proclaim her bravery when we recall that she started out with an invalid husband and seven children to this far western land where everything was undeveloped and she was unable to speak the language of the people among whom she was expected to live."*

Owen, who survived that week on the Mississippi as a thirteen-year-old boy, never spoke of it in his known dictated history. The Peters letter — sent to his widow — is the document that gives the precise dates, names, and days of the week of each death.

## The Family Line Forward

Owen and Jane's daughter **Ellenor Mary Owens**, born 1858/1859, married **John Joseph Ward**. Their son **John Welton Ward** married **Beatrice Ann Prouse**. Their son **Ernest Trulock Ward** married **Anna Elizabeth Barker**. Their daughter **Marian Rula Ward** married **Clarence Jordan Naylor**. Their daughter **Cynthia Ann Naylor** is Erik Taylor's mother — making Owen and Jane Parsons Owens Erik's 4× great-grandparents.

The Nauvoo Temple Jane saw as a little girl on her father's shoulders. The cholera river Owen survived at thirteen. The dance where he danced in his bare feet and first saw Jane. The first wooden roof in Willard. Braided wheat hats, linsey cloth, and the last load of hay the day before the wedding. Twenty-five years as Relief Society counselor. Milk wagons to Pleasant View. A horse that was "not a good looker."

All of it is in the record.
