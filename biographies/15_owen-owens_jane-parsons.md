## Wales: Farming, Slate, and a New Faith

Owen Owens was born 21 December 1836 in Merionethshire, North Wales — a rugged region of farming and slate quarrying in the mountains of northwest Wales. He had a twin sister, Catherine, who died when she was twelve. Owen was the son of William Owens and Eleanor Evans Owens, a family deeply rooted in Welsh rural life.

William Owens was a substantial farmer who rented a large tract of land — paying ninety pounds per year — on which he kept twenty milk cows and a thousand to two thousand head of sheep. The family were members of the Baptist Church, though William was not known as a particularly ardent participant.

In 1848, something changed the family's life forever. LDS missionaries brought the gospel to Merionethshire, and the entire William Owens family accepted baptism at Festiniog, Wales. The decision to join the Church triggered an equally momentous decision: to emigrate to Zion in America. This would prove one of the most costly journeys of their lives.

---

## The Catastrophic Crossing: Cholera, 1849

In spring 1849, the William Owens family — with nine members — departed Liverpool for New Orleans, intending to make their way to the gathering place of the Saints. Their story is one of the most heartbreaking in this family history.

On the Mississippi River, cholera struck with devastating speed. In a single week:
- **Jane** (one of the children) died 7 May 1849 and was buried on the riverbank
- **William** (son) died 8 May 1849
- **Alice** died 8 May 1849 — the same day as her brother
- **Eleanor Owens** (the mother) died 9 May 1849
- **William Owens** (the father) died 12 May 1849
- **Richard Owens** died 18 May 1849

Six of the nine family members died in less than two weeks, buried along the banks of the Mississippi River. Only three survivors continued: Owen (age 13), an older sister (age 20), and an older brother (age 24).

Owen Owens, a thirteen-year-old boy, arrived in Utah an orphan who had just watched most of his family perish.

---

## Starting Over in Zion

The survivors traveled by wagon to Council Bluffs, Iowa, then equipped themselves with one yoke of cows, two yokes of oxen, and headed for the Salt Lake Valley. They arrived in time for the fall conference of 1849.

That first winter, Owen and his older brother and sister lived in a dugout across the Jordan River in a Welsh colony. The older two soon married and left Owen to fend for himself.

Owen's early Utah years were years of labor and learning:
- **Spring 1850**: Hired by John Mercer for eight months at $8/month for general farm work south of Salt Lake City
- **Fall 1850**: Went to Sanpete County with Captain Dan Jones for a year of farm labor
- **Winter 1851**: Lived with his married sister in Salt Lake City and attended school
- **Summer 1852**: Farmed for John Bauch in Salt Lake City
- **February 1853**: Moved to American Fork — where he met Jane Parsons

---

## Jane Parsons: From Nauvoo to Utah

Jane Parsons had a completely different but equally extraordinary story.

She was born 13 October 1842 in Nauvoo, Illinois. Her parents, William Parsons (born 27 March 1807, Worcestershire, England) and Mary Jenkins Parsons (born 28 September 1813, Herefordshire, England) had been Welsh Methodists who converted to a sect called the "Ranters" before an even more profound conversion: *"when Brother Wilford Woodruff came to their village in the spring of 1840, as a traveling Elder with the gospel message, their hearts were prepared to receive it."* The entire Parsons branch — some 600 members — was baptized by Wilford Woodruff in March 1840 and left England that September on the sailing vessel *North America*. As Jane would later record: *"This was the first ship load of saints that left England for the gospel's sake."*

Jane's earliest memories were of Nauvoo: *"I remember being in a large crowd of people and my father letting me on his shoulder"* as she gazed at the faces carved in limestone on the Nauvoo Temple. She also remembered the unsettling sight of armed men drilling as the Nauvoo Legion organized against mob threats.

After the Prophet Joseph Smith's martyrdom, the Parsons family joined the westward exodus. They endured Winter Quarters, then moved near Council Bluffs, and finally in June 1852 — with one wagon, two yoke of oxen, and one yoke of cows — made the overland journey. They arrived in Utah 23 August 1852, spent a week in Salt Lake City, and settled in American Fork.

There, in 1853, Jane Parsons and Owen Owens found each other.

---

## A Marriage and a Move to Willard

Owen and Jane were married in the Endowment House in Salt Lake City on 2 October 1857. Owen was 20; Jane was 14. They were members of a tight community of Welsh and English Latter-day Saints.

In April 1859, the Owenses relocated to **Willard, Box Elder County, Utah** — a community that would be their home for the rest of their lives. Jane's parents came too. Owen and his father-in-law William Parsons purchased the Ira Parks farm together: 28 acres in Block 18 of the Willard Survey.

Owen built a log house on a lot he purchased. With nails costing a dollar per pound, the floor was laid with wooden pegs. The roof was wooden — the first and only one in Willard in 1860.

---

## Pioneer Life in Willard

Owen Owens worked as a farmer throughout his life, and his success supported a large family. For seven years he ran a milk wagon from Willard to Pleasant View. He served as **assistant superintendent in the Sunday School** and as a **bishop's counselor** in the Willard Ward.

To Owen and Jane Parsons were born nine children. In February 1871, Owen took a second wife, Martha Waite, in the practice of plural marriage. To that union were born eight more children.

One of Owen and Jane's children was **Ellenor Mary Owens**, born 14 March 1859 in American Fork — she who would marry John Joseph Ward and become Erik Taylor's 2× great-grandmother (see Biography #22).

---

## Jane's Own Words: Written in 1915

When she was 72 years old, Jane wrote a remarkable history for her grandson William W. Owens in January 1915. In it she described childhood memories of Nauvoo, the temple, the drilling of the Nauvoo Legion, the long trek west, and frontier life in American Fork and Willard. She described herself as having very little formal schooling — *"I do not remember learning my letters. I had a book called Webster's Elementary Spelling Book"* — yet she wrote with clarity and feeling.

*"My parents were always leaders in every community where they lived,"* she noted, and by her account, the same could be said of Owen and Jane themselves.

---

## Deaths and Legacy

Owen Owens, after being confined to his bed with pneumonia for four days, died at 11:15 p.m. on Friday, 13 June 1913, in Willard. He was 76 years old. His funeral was held at the Willard Tabernacle. The hymns chosen — "Come, Come, Ye Saints," "I Know that My Redeemer Lives," and "By Their Fruits Shall Ye Know Them" — were, as his family history notes, *"an appropriate eulogy to this illustrious forebear."*

Jane Parsons Owens survived until 21 July 1922, dying in Willard at age 79.

Owen's biography was given to his grandson William W. Owens on 30 December 1912, just six months before Owen's death — a final gift of memory from a man who had survived extraordinary loss and built an extraordinary life.
