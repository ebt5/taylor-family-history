## From Trowbridge and Weymouth: Two English Converts

William Joseph Taylor was born 7 July 1832 in Trowbridge, Wiltshire — a textile-manufacturing town in the west of England. His father was William Taylor and his mother Winifred Hewett. In his early years he heard the LDS gospel, found it readily appealing, *"and immediately he applied for baptism"* — as his granddaughter Mary Taylor Dahlquist recorded. Shortly thereafter he was called on a mission to Ireland, making him a missionary in his twenties before he was a husband.

Mary Bowring was born 5 August 1827 in Weymouth, Dorset — a coastal town in the southwest of England. Her parents Joseph Daw Bowring and Jane Cook Bowring were from Marcus (likely Maiden Newton or Marcus Cerne area), Dorsetshire. Mary was baptized 27 September 1849 and was rebaptized 16 April 1857.

Mary's own voice survives in a brief autobiographical note: *"On August 5, 1827, I was born into the family of Joseph and Jane C[ook Bowring]..."* — a rare first-person glimpse into her origins.

---

## Marriage and the Voyage West

William and Mary married on 30 January 1853 in England. Within days, the spirit of gathering moved them. They set sail on **5 February 1853** aboard the ship **Jersey** bound for the valleys of the mountains.

As Mary Taylor Dahlquist recorded: *"From St. Louis they came by ox team across the plains arriving in Salt Lake Valley on the 27th day of September, 1853 having been eight months on the way from their home in England."*

They arrived in Provo on 4 January 1854. It was here that their lives would unfold entirely.

A rich additional record of the ship voyage has been preserved. According to research by Ruby T. Olson, a manuscript in the Church Historians Office describes the crossing from England to New Orleans of a company of 314 Saints aboard the sailing vessel — the very voyage that brought William and Mary to America.

---

## Provo Pioneers: Woolen Mills and the School

Almost immediately upon settling in Provo, William Taylor was called into service by the Church's colonizing vision. **President Brigham Young asked Brother Taylor to go down and oversee the carding department and help generally with the manufacture of the woolens** at the Provo Woolen Mills, which were then under construction. William supervised the carding department — one of the skilled textile operations at the heart of Utah's early industrial effort.

Meanwhile, Mary stepped up with equal purpose. Unable to simply depend on William's mill wages to support the family, **Sister Taylor opened up a public school in her own home**. That school grew — and *"later it was known as the Seminary."* Mary taught while also coloring, carding, and spinning wool for the community, and she made denim overalls and jumpers for the East Co-op Store. She later made temple suits and other religious garments. It was a life of constant, productive labor.

---

## William: Peace Officer, Plural Marriage, and the Blackhawk War

In 1860, William was elected **peace officer of Provo** — a frontier law-enforcement role in a territory that was simultaneously a theocracy and a military buffer. In 1865–1866 he **enlisted in defense of the Saints against the Utes during the Blackhawk War** — Utah's largest and most costly conflict with Native Americans.

At the close of hostilities in August 1867, William operated a **tannery** — where hides were cured and made into shoes. As his granddaughter noted with wry affection, the shoes were imperfect: *"wet weather making them loose and flabby and dry weather causing them to become hard and dry."*

William complied with plural marriage, taking a second wife **Rebecca Harris** (niece of Martin Harris, one of the Three Witnesses to the Book of Mormon). Two children were born to Rebecca and William.

William was also called as a **home missionary** and was in the first organization of the **34th Quorum of Seventies**.

He was a member of the Provo Woolen Mills organization and was called by Brigham Young to supervise manufacturing there. He also fought in the Blackhawk War.

One of his most enduring acts: William planted an oak tree on the corner of 600 West and 100 South in Provo. Family tradition holds he had brought the acorn across the plains from England. That oak became known as the **Pioneer Tree** — protected by the state of Utah.

## Seven Children, then Loss

William and Mary had seven children together:
1. Joseph (born ca. 1854–55)
2. Mary Ann (born ca. 1857)
3. William Weldon Taylor (born ca. 1860) — *Erik's great-grandfather*
4. [Additional children]
5–7. Others through the 1860s

William Joseph Taylor died suddenly and young — of **brain fever** — on **18 May 1869**. He was 37 years old. He left Mary with seven children, *"the eldest scarcely 15 and a baby one year and eight months."* Rebecca was left with two children ages one and three.

Mary later said the Lord truly fulfilled his promise to her: *"Although we shared the privations which was common lot of the pioneers, still we were never hungry."*

---

## Mary: Thirty-Two Years as a Widow and Matriarch

Mary Bowring Taylor outlived her husband by thirty-two years. She took in William's two children by Rebecca Harris as well — raising nine children total on the frontier.

After William's death, Mary and her oldest son Joseph managed the farm. They gleaned in neighboring fields. Mary continued spinning, weaving, teaching, and making garments for the community. She also raised two children from William's plural wife.

Mary Bowring Taylor died 27 March 1901 in Provo, Utah. Cemetery records have her death as 28 March (family records say 27 March). She is buried in the Provo City Cemetery.

Her life story was compiled in 1984 by Maxine Taylor Bleak Hill and Eleanor Olsen Pratt: *"A 1984 Compilation of the history of William Joseph Taylor, Jr and Abby Jane Scott"* — a family history volume that includes information about Mary Bowring's line and the extended Taylor family.

---

## The Pioneer Tree

The oak tree William Taylor planted at 600 West and 100 South in Provo still stands (or stood until recent years) as a physical link to the family's pioneer past. An acorn carried across the plains, planted in the rocky Utah soil — and a tree that outlived the man who planted it by more than a century. It remains one of the family's most tangible connections to William Joseph Taylor.
